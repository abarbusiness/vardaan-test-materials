10.
Sublimation: Direct conversion of a solid into gas on heating without passing through the liquid state. [1 Mark]
Kinetic Theory: On heating, particles gain extreme kinetic energy and vibrate vigorously, completely overcoming the intermolecular forces of attraction directly from the solid state to break free into the gaseous state. [1 Mark]
Example: Naphthalene balls, Camphor, or Dry ice (Any one). [1 Mark]

∛(27) = 3 [1 Mark]

∛(0.064) = ∛(64/1000) = 4/10 = 0.4
Expression = 3 + 0.2 + 0.4 = 3.6 [1 Mark]
12.
Solids: Strongest intermolecular forces and negligible intermolecular spaces. Particles are closely packed and can only vibrate at their mean positions, giving a definite shape and volume. [1½ Marks]
Gases: Negligible intermolecular forces and large intermolecular spaces. Particles have high kinetic energy and move freely in all directions, acquiring the shape and volume of the container. [1½ Marks]

14.
Occupies space: Take an empty balloon and blow air into it. It inflates and increases in size, showing that the air (matter) inside occupies space. [1½ Marks]
Has mass: Place the empty balloon on a digital weighing scale and record its mass. Then weigh the inflated balloon. The inflated balloon will weigh slightly more, proving air has mass. [1½ Marks]
(Alternate valid experiments, like the inverted glass in water, are acceptable if fully explained).
15.
Prime factorization of 8788:
8788 = 2 × 2 × 13 × 13 × 13 [1½ Marks]
Triplets: (13 × 13 × 13) are in a triplet. The factors 2 × 2 are left over. [½ Mark]
To make the quotient a perfect cube, we must eliminate the 2 × 2.
Smallest number to divide by = 2 × 2 = 4. [1 Mark]
16.
Latent heat of fusion: The amount of heat energy required to change a unit mass of a solid into a liquid at its melting point without any change in temperature. [1½ Marks]
Reason: The heat supplied during melting does not increase kinetic energy (so temp stays constant); instead, it is utilized as potential energy to completely overcome the intermolecular forces of attraction between solid particles. [1½ Marks]

20.
Sequential Changes:
1. Ice at -10°C to 0°C: As heat is absorbed, the kinetic energy of ice molecules increases and temperature rises to 0°C. State remains solid. [1 Mark]
2. Melting at 0°C: Temperature remains constant. The heat supplied (latent heat) is used to overcome the strong intermolecular forces of attraction holding the ice lattice together. Kinetic energy is constant, but potential energy increases. Solid ice turns to liquid water. [2 Marks]
3. Water at 0°C to 100°C: Kinetic energy of water molecules increases continuously, causing the temperature to rise up to 100°C. State remains liquid. [1 Mark]
4. Boiling at 100°C: Temperature remains constant. Heat (latent heat of vaporization) is utilized to completely break all remaining intermolecular forces. Kinetic energy is constant. Liquid water transitions into gaseous steam. [1 Mark]

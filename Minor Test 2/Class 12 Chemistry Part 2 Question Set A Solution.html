SECTION A: Colligative Properties & Vapour Pressure
1.
Colligative properties of dilute solutions depend strictly on which of the following?
(A) The chemical identity of the solute particles
(B) The physical state of the solute particles
(C) The number of solute particles present in the solution
(D) Both the nature and number of solute particles
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
By fundamental definition, colligative properties (such as relative lowering of vapour pressure, elevation of boiling point, depression of freezing point, and osmotic pressure) depend strictly on the ratio or number of solute particles relative to solvent particles, and are completely independent of the solute's chemical identity or nature.
2.
For highly dilute solutions (where n2 << n1), the formula for Relative Lowering of Vapour Pressure (RLVP) simplifies extensively to calculate the molar mass of the solute. The correct simplified formula is:
(A) (P10 - P1) / P10 = (w2 × M1) / (w1 × M2)
(B) (P10 - P1) / P10 = (w1 × M2) / (w2 × M1)
(C) (P10 - P1) / P10 = (n1 × n2) / (w1 × w2)
(D) (P10 - P1) / P1 = (w2 × M1) / (w1 × M2)
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (A)
RLVP = x2 = n2 / (n1 + n2).
For a highly dilute solution, n2 << n1, so the denominator (n1 + n2) is approximated to n1.
Thus, RLVP ≈ n2 / n1 = (w2 / M2) / (w1 / M1) = (w2 × M1) / (w1 × M2).
3.
According to Raoult's law and boiling point concepts, a liquid boils precisely when:
(A) Its temperature reaches 100°C
(B) Its vapour pressure becomes equal to the atmospheric pressure
(C) The vapour pressure of the solute exceeds that of the solvent
(D) The atmospheric pressure is completely reduced to zero
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
The boiling point of any liquid is scientifically defined as the exact temperature at which the liquid's vapour pressure equals the external (atmospheric) pressure surrounding it. This is why adding a non-volatile solute (which lowers vapour pressure) demands a higher temperature to reach boiling.
4.
Boiling point of water at 750 mm Hg is 99.63°C. How much sucrose (Molar mass = 342 g/mol) is to be added to 500 g of water such that it boils at exactly 100°C? (Given Kb for water is 0.52 K kg mol-1).
(A) 121.67 g
(B) 243.34 g
(C) 171.00 g
(D) 342.00 g
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (A)
Elevation required, ΔTb = 100°C - 99.63°C = 0.37 K.
Formula: ΔTb = Kb × (w2 × 1000) / (M2 × w1).
0.37 = 0.52 × (w2 × 1000) / (342 × 500).
w2 = (0.37 × 342 × 500) / (0.52 × 1000) = 63270 / 520 = 121.67 g.
5.
The freezing point of a substance is scientifically defined as the temperature at which:
(A) The solid phase begins to melt rapidly
(B) The solution phase becomes perfectly transparent
(C) The solid phase and liquid phase have the exact same vapour pressure
(D) The vapour pressure of the liquid phase reaches 1 atm
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
Freezing point is strictly defined as the temperature at which the liquid and solid states of a substance are in dynamic equilibrium, meaning both phases share the exact identical vapour pressure.
6.
What is the standard unit of the Ebullioscopic constant (Kb) and Cryoscopic constant (Kf)?
(A) K mol kg-1
(B) K kg mol-1
(C) K L mol-1
(D) mol kg K-1
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
Since ΔT = K × m, rearranging gives K = ΔT / m.
The unit of ΔT is Kelvin (K), and the unit of molality (m) is mol/kg (or mol kg-1).
Therefore, Unit of K = K / (mol kg-1) = K kg mol-1.
7.
The formula to calculate the molar mass of a non-volatile solute (M2) utilizing the depression of freezing point (ΔTf) is:
(A) M2 = (1000 × w1 × Kf) / (ΔTf × w2)
(B) M2 = (1000 × w2 × ΔTf) / (Kf × w1)
(C) M2 = (1000 × w2 × Kf) / (ΔTf × w1)
(D) M2 = (w2 × Kf) / (1000 × ΔTf × w1)
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
Formula for FP depression: ΔTf = Kf × m.
Expanding molality (m): ΔTf = Kf × [ (w2 / M2) / (w1 / 1000) ].
Rearranging to solve for M2 gives: M2 = (1000 × w2 × Kf) / (ΔTf × w1).
8.
Based on Raoult's law, the Relative Lowering of Vapour Pressure for a solution is exactly equal to the:
(A) Mole fraction of the solvent (x1)
(B) Mole fraction of the solute (x2)
(C) Molality of the solution (m)
(D) Molarity of the solution (M)
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
According to Raoult's law for a solution containing a non-volatile solute, the relative lowering of vapour pressure ( (P10 - P1) / P10 ) is fundamentally equal to the mole fraction of the solute in the solution (x2).
9.
In the phenomenon of osmosis, the spontaneous flow of solvent molecules through a Semi-Permeable Membrane (SPM) occurs from:
(A) A concentrated solution to a dilute solution
(B) A pure solvent to a solution
(C) A solution to a pure solvent
(D) The solute phase to the solvent phase
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
Osmosis is the spontaneous net movement of solvent molecules across a semi-permeable membrane. The flow always moves from a region of higher solvent concentration (pure solvent or dilute solution) to a region of lower solvent concentration (concentrated solution) to equalize concentrations.
10.
Reverse Osmosis (RO), utilized globally for the desalination of seawater, occurs exclusively when the external pressure applied to the solution side is:
(A) Exactly equal to the osmotic pressure
(B) Less than the osmotic pressure
(C) Larger than the osmotic pressure
(D) Equal to the atmospheric pressure
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
Osmotic pressure (π) is the exact pressure required to STOP osmosis. If an external pressure greater than the osmotic pressure (P > π) is applied to the concentrated solution (like seawater), the natural flow reverses. Pure solvent is forced out of the solution through the membrane, which is the principle of RO.
SECTION B: Osmotic Pressure & Biomolecules
11.
The osmotic pressure (π) of a solution is mathematically proportional to which concentration term at a given fixed temperature (T)?
(A) Molality (m)
(B) Mole fraction (x)
(C) Molarity (C)
(D) Mass percentage (% w/w)
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
The Van't Hoff equation for osmotic pressure is π = CRT, where C represents the Molarity (moles of solute per liter of solution). Thus, at constant temperature, π is directly proportional to molarity.
12.
Why is the measurement of osmotic pressure highly preferred over other colligative properties for determining the molar mass of biomolecules (like proteins and polymers)?
(A) It relies on measuring boiling point elevation which is highly accurate.
(B) It is measured at room temperature, preventing the denaturation of proteins.
(C) It utilizes molality, which is completely independent of temperature changes.
(D) The resulting magnitudes are incredibly small, making calculations easier.
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
Biomolecules like proteins are thermally unstable and easily denature at boiling temperatures (rendering ΔTb useless). Osmotic pressure is measured at standard room temperature. Additionally, even for very dilute macromolecule solutions, osmotic pressure yields a large, easily measurable magnitude, unlike other properties.
13.
A 0.9% (mass/volume) sodium chloride solution is safely injected intravenously because it is isotonic with human blood plasma. Two solutions are termed "isotonic" when they have the exact same:
(A) Elevation in boiling point
(B) Molality
(C) Osmotic pressure at a given temperature
(D) Vapour pressure at 100°C
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
Two solutions separated by a semi-permeable membrane are termed "isotonic" if they exert exactly the same osmotic pressure at a given temperature. Consequently, no net osmosis occurs between them.
14.
If a living cell is placed inside a hypertonic solution (a solution with a higher concentration), what will happen to the cell?
(A) It will absorb water, swell, and potentially burst (Hemolysis).
(B) It will remain completely unchanged.
(C) It will lose water and shrink (Plasmolysis).
(D) The cell membrane will dissolve instantly.
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
A hypertonic solution has a higher solute concentration than the fluid inside the cell. To balance the concentration, solvent (water) will spontaneously flow out of the cell across the membrane into the surrounding solution, causing the cell to shrink and undergo plasmolysis.
15.
Hemolysis occurs when a red blood cell absorbs water, swells, and bursts. This phenomenon is exclusively observed when the cell is placed inside a:
(A) Hypertonic solution
(B) Isotonic solution
(C) Hypotonic solution
(D) Saturated solution
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
A hypotonic solution has a lower solute concentration compared to the cell interior. Osmosis drives water to move from the hypotonic solution (high water concentration) into the cell, causing it to swell up and ultimately burst (hemolysis).
16.
200 cm3 of an aqueous solution of a protein contains 1.26 g of the protein. The osmotic pressure of this solution at 300 K is found to be 2.57 × 10-3 bar. What is the molar mass of the protein? (R = 0.083 L bar K-1 mol-1).
(A) 31,050 g/mol
(B) 61,038 g/mol
(C) 122,000 g/mol
(D) 6,104 g/mol
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
Volume V = 200 cm3 = 0.200 L.
Using the formula π = CRT = (w2 × R × T) / (M2 × V).
Rearranging to find M2: M2 = (w2 × R × T) / (π × V).
M2 = (1.26 × 0.083 × 300) / (2.57 × 10-3 × 0.200).
M2 = 31.374 / 0.000514 = 61038.9 g/mol ≈ 61,038 g/mol.
17.
The Van't Hoff factor (i) was introduced by Jacobus Henricus van 't Hoff primarily to mathematically account for:
(A) The exact atmospheric pressure at sea level
(B) The extent of dissociation or association of solute particles in a solution
(C) The variation of the universal gas constant (R)
(D) The volatility of the solvent
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
Formulas for colligative properties assume solute particles don't interact. However, ionic compounds dissociate (multiply) and some organics associate (combine). The Van't Hoff factor (i) was introduced specifically to correct the calculations by tracking the true extent of this dissociation or association.
18.
When calculating the normal molar mass versus the abnormal molar mass, the defining formula for the Van't Hoff factor (i) is:
(A) i = Abnormal Molar Mass / Normal Molar Mass
(B) i = Normal Molar Mass / Abnormal Molar Mass
(C) i = Calculated Colligative Property / Observed Colligative Property
(D) i = Normal Molar Mass × Abnormal Molar Mass
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
By definition, i = Observed Colligative Property / Calculated Colligative Property. Because colligative properties are inversely proportional to the molar mass of the solute, the ratio for molar masses flips: i = Normal (Calculated) Molar Mass / Abnormal (Observed) Molar Mass.
19.
Some organic molecules (e.g., Acetic acid in benzene) associate to form dimers due to hydrogen bonding. For such solutes undergoing association, the calculated molar mass derived from colligative properties is:
(A) Exactly equal to the true normal molar mass
(B) Lower than the true normal molar mass
(C) Higher than the true normal molar mass
(D) Completely unpredictable
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
When molecules associate (like dimerizing), the total number of distinct particles in solution drops. This causes the observed colligative property value to be smaller than expected. Since Molar Mass is inversely proportional to the colligative property, a smaller property value results in a calculated (abnormal) molar mass that is higher than the true value.
20.
For non-electrolytes such as Glucose, Urea, and Sucrose, which neither dissociate nor associate in solution, the value of the Van't Hoff factor (i) is:
(A) 0
(B) Exactly 1
(C) Greater than 1
(D) Less than 1
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
Since non-electrolytes remain entirely intact when dissolved, one molecule added yields exactly one particle in solution. The number of particles does not change. Therefore, Observed property = Calculated property, resulting in a Van't Hoff factor of exactly 1.
SECTION C: Van't Hoff Factor & Abnormal Molar Mass
21.
To correct the formulas for colligative properties when dealing with electrolytes (which undergo dissociation), one must multiply the calculated property by the Van't Hoff factor. Which of the following is the CORRECT modified formula for the Elevation of Boiling Point?
(A) ΔTb = Kb × m / i
(B) ΔTb = i × Kb × m
(C) ΔTb = (i - 1) × Kb × m
(D) ΔTb = Kb × m
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
To apply the correction for the actual number of particles present due to dissociation or association, the entire theoretical colligative property formula must be multiplied directly by the Van't Hoff factor (i). Thus, ΔTb = i × Kb × m.
22.
For a weak electrolyte that dissociates into 'n' total ions, the correct mathematical relationship between its degree of dissociation (α) and the Van't Hoff factor (i) is:
(A) α = (1 - i) / (1 - n)
(B) α = (i - 1) / (n - 1)
(C) α = (n - 1) / (i - 1)
(D) α = i / n
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
Let initial moles = 1. If degree of dissociation is α, moles left = 1 - α. Moles of ions formed = nα.
Total particles = (1 - α) + nα = 1 + α(n - 1).
Since i = Total particles / Initial particles = [1 + α(n - 1)] / 1.
Rearranging: i - 1 = α(n - 1) ⇒ α = (i - 1) / (n - 1).
23.
A specific salt gives exactly 3 ions upon complete dissociation in water. If its degree of dissociation (α) at a given concentration is found to be 0.8, what is the calculated value of its Van't Hoff factor (i)?
(A) 1.6
(B) 3.0
(C) 2.6
(D) 0.8
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
Using the derived relation: i = 1 + α(n - 1).
Given n = 3 (ions produced) and α = 0.8.
i = 1 + 0.8(3 - 1) = 1 + 0.8(2).
i = 1 + 1.6 = 2.6.
24.
An unknown organic solute undergoes dimerization (n=2) in a specific solvent. If the experimental Van't Hoff factor (i) for the solution is determined to be 0.6, what is its degree of association (α)?
(A) 0.8
(B) 0.4
(C) 0.6
(D) 1.2
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (A)
For association, the relationship is i = 1 - α + (α/n).
For dimerization, n = 2. So, i = 1 - α + (α/2) = 1 - (α/2).
Given i = 0.6: 0.6 = 1 - (α/2).
α/2 = 1 - 0.6 = 0.4.
α = 0.4 × 2 = 0.8.
25.
Which of the following represents the CORRECT modified formula for calculating Osmotic Pressure (π) for an electrolyte solution?
(A) π = C R T
(B) π = (C R T) / i
(C) π = i C R T
(D) π = i m R T
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
The normal equation for osmotic pressure is π = CRT (where C is molarity). For an electrolyte, the number of particles changes, so we multiply the entire right side by the Van't Hoff factor (i) to get the correct modified equation: π = i C R T.

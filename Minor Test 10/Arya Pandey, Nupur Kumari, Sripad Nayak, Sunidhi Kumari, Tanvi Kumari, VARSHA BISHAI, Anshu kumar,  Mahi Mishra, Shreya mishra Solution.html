SOLUTIONS & MARKING SCHEME - MINOR TEST (MIT 10)

Class: 10 CBSE | Subject: English Lang. & Lit. | Max Marks: 50

SECTION A: GRAMMAR (10 Marks)
Q1. Fill in the blanks with determiners: (a) ____ (some/any) sugar (b) ____ (Both/All) the students
Answer: 2 Marks
(a) any
(b) All
Marking: 1 mark for each correct answer. No partial marks for spelling errors.
Q2. Fill in the blanks with correct tenses: (a) the thief ____ (escape) (b) Anil usually ____ (make) money...
Answer: 2 Marks
(a) had escaped (Past Perfect is used for the earlier of two past actions).
(b) makes (Simple Present is used for habitual actions indicated by 'usually').
Marking: 1 mark for each correct tense formulation.
Q3. Fill in the blanks with modals: (a) You ____ (must/ought) to be polite... (b) there ____ (would/should) be no limit...
Answer: 2 Marks
(a) ought (Followed by 'to', indicating moral obligation).
(b) would (Expressing conditional certainty in the past).
Marking: 1 mark for each correct modal.
Q4. Error Correction: "A little flattery help in making friends easily."
Answer: 2 Marks
Error: help
Correction: helps (Subject-verb agreement; 'flattery' is singular).
Marking: 1 mark for identifying the error, 1 mark for correct correction.
Q5. Error Correction: "He was the most trusting person I have ever met."
Answer: 2 Marks
Error: have
Correction: had (Tense consistency; the main clause is in past tense 'was').
Marking: 1 mark for identifying the error, 1 mark for correct correction.
SECTION B: LITERATURE SHORT ANSWERS (15 Marks)
Q6. Why did Hari Singh choose Anil to be his next victim? State two reasons.
Answer: 3 Marks
Hari Singh chose Anil because Anil looked easy-going, kind, and simple enough for his purpose. Secondly, Hari Singh had not had much 'luck' of late and felt he could easily win this young man's confidence through flattery to eventually rob him.
Marking: 1.5 marks for each valid reason. (Easy-going/simple nature + need to win confidence).
Q7. "Anil made money by fits and starts." Explain the meaning of this phrase in the context of Anil's profession.
Answer: 3 Marks
The phrase "by fits and starts" means irregularly or unsteadily. In the context of Anil's profession, it meant he did not have a regular, fixed income. He wrote for magazines, so he would borrow one week, lend the next, and worry about his next cheque. When the cheque arrived, he would immediately go out and celebrate.
Marking: 1 mark for defining the phrase. 2 marks for relating it accurately to Anil's writing profession and irregular income.
Q8. Why was it more difficult for Hari Singh to rob Anil than to rob a greedy man?
Answer: 3 Marks
Hari Singh found it difficult to rob Anil because Anil was careless and extremely trusting. A greedy man can afford to be robbed, but robbing a careless and trusting man takes all the pleasure out of the work, as the victim might not even notice he has been robbed, rendering the thief's skills meaningless.
Marking: 1.5 marks for mentioning Anil's careless/trusting nature. 1.5 marks for contrasting it with a greedy man/taking pleasure out of the theft.
Q9. What did Hari Singh experience when he was standing alone on the deserted platform after missing the Lucknow Express?
Answer: 3 Marks
Standing alone on the platform, Hari Singh felt lost and conflicted. He had nowhere to go as he had no friends and avoided hotels. A sense of guilt washed over him. He realized that in the short time, he had learned about human faces—that a trusting man like Anil would not show anger or fear, but a touch of profound sadness at the loss of trust.
Marking: 1 mark for physical state (nowhere to go/no friends). 2 marks for his psychological realization about Anil's reaction (sadness for loss of trust).
Q10. How did Hari Singh know that Anil had realized the money was stolen, even though Anil said nothing?
Answer: 3 Marks
Hari Singh knew Anil had discovered the theft because when Anil gave him a fifty-rupee note the next morning, the note was still wet from the previous night's rain. Despite knowing the truth, Anil did not show any anger or hand him over to the police; instead, he casually mentioned they would start writing sentences.
Marking: 2 marks for identifying the wet note. 1 mark for mentioning Anil's silent, forgiving reaction.

Q12. Extract: "When the train had gone, I found myself standing alone..." (a) Why standing alone? (b) Philosophy on friends? (c) Why avoid hotel? (d) Word for 'empty'.
Answer: 5 Marks
(a) The speaker (Hari Singh) was standing alone because he had hesitated and intentionally missed the Lucknow Express in a moment of inner conflict after stealing Anil's money. (1)

(b) His philosophy was that friends were more of a trouble than a help, particularly for someone in his profession (thievery), as they could expose him or demand a share. (1)

(c) He avoided staying at a small hotel near the station because he did not want to attract attention or make anyone curious about a young boy carrying a large sum of money late at night. (1)

(d) The word in the extract that means 'empty of people' is "deserted". (2)
Marking: 1+1+1+2 = 5 Marks. Direct and accurate answers required.
Q13. Character Sketch of Anil highlighting trust, compassion, and forgiveness.
Answer: 5 Marks
Anil is arguably the most remarkable and grounded character in Ruskin Bond's "The Thief's Story." He is a 25-year-old struggling writer who makes money by fits and starts. His physical appearance is tall, lean, and unassuming, perfectly reflecting his easy-going and simple nature.

Anil's most defining traits are his profound compassion and boundless trust. Despite knowing that Hari Singh lied about knowing how to cook, Anil didn't throw him out; instead, he taught him to cook, write his name, and promised to teach him whole sentences. He gave Hari the keys to the door, showing absolute trust, which ultimately burdened Hari's conscience.

Furthermore, Anil's capacity for forgiveness is extraordinary. When he discovered the theft through the wet currency notes, he neither handed Hari over to the police nor humiliated him. Instead, he absorbed the betrayal silently, offering Hari fifty rupees and a promise of regular pay. This silent forgiveness proved to be the catalyst for Hari's complete moral transformation.
Marking Scheme: Content - 3 Marks (Must include profession, simple nature, teaching aspect, absolute trust, and the silent forgiveness act). Expression - 2 Marks (Coherence, grammar, and vocabulary).
Q14. Conscience stopped Hari Singh. Are human beings inherently bad or do circumstances force them?
Answer: 5 Marks
Hari Singh's inability to board the Lucknow Express and his decision to return the stolen money strongly suggest that no human being is inherently bad. Deep down, everyone possesses a moral compass and a conscience.

At just fifteen years old, Hari Singh was an experienced thief. It was not inherent evil, but likely poverty, lack of guidance, and tough circumstances that pushed him into a life of crime. However, Anil's unadulterated trust, kindness, and the prospect of education awakened the dormant goodness within him. When he stood on the deserted platform, the realization of what he was losing—the respect of a good man and the chance at a dignified, literate life—outweighed the greed for six hundred rupees.

The story beautifully validates that while harsh circumstances might lead individuals astray, genuine love, trust, and compassion have the power to reform even the most hardened hearts, proving that goodness is intrinsic to human nature.
Marking Scheme: Content - 3 Marks (Justifying the stance using Hari's inner conflict, role of circumstances vs. inherent goodness, Anil's role). Expression - 2 Marks.
Q15. "Education and literacy can bring a profound change in a person's mindset." Justify with reference to Hari Singh.
Answer: 5 Marks
The statement holds true in the context of "The Thief's Story." For Hari Singh, stealing was a temporary means of survival, a petty crime that brought him a few hundred rupees. However, Anil's promise to teach him how to read and write whole sentences planted a seed of ambition and dignity in his mind.

While sitting in the maidan after the theft, Hari Singh reflected on his actions. He realized that stealing was easy, but being a "really big man, a clever and respected man" was something entirely different. This realization was rooted directly in the prospect of education. He understood that literacy could open doors to limitless opportunities, far exceeding the petty amount he had stolen.

It was this profound shift in mindset—from that of a short-sighted thief to a young boy desiring respectability through education—that drove him back to Anil. Thus, education acted as the ultimate tool for his moral reformation.
Marking Scheme: Content - 3 Marks (Connecting education to dignity/respect, the realization at the maidan, shift from petty thief to ambitious boy). Expression - 2 Marks.

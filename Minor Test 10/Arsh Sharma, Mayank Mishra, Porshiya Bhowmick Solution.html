SECTION A: ENGLISH LANGUAGE (25 Marks)
Q1. Formal Letter Writing (Complaint OR Editor)
(a) Complaint regarding a defective smartphone to the Customer Service Manager.
OR
(b) Letter to the Editor regarding road accidents caused by underage and reckless driving.
Answer / Value Points: 10 Marks
Option (a) - Letter of Complaint (Value Points):
  • Format: Sender's Address, Date, Receiver's Designation & Address, Subject, Salutation, Subscription, Signature.
  • Intro: Details of purchase (Model number, date of purchase, invoice number, store location).
  • Body: Description of the problem (e.g., battery draining fast, screen flickering, heating up). Mention the unresponsive behavior of the local store manager.
  • Conclusion: Requesting prompt replacement under warranty terms or scheduling a service visit.

Option (b) - Letter to the Editor (Value Points):
  • Format: Sender's Address, Date, Receiver's Address (The Editor, National Daily), Subject, Salutation, Subscription.
  • Intro: "Through the columns of your esteemed newspaper, I wish to draw the attention of the authorities..."
  • Body: Highlighting the rise in accidents. Pointing out the root cause: teenagers driving without licenses, lack of parental supervision, speeding for thrill.
  • Conclusion/Suggestions: Strict checking by traffic police, heavy fines for parents of underage drivers, awareness campaigns in schools.
Marking Scheme (ICSE Format 10 Marks):
- Format (3 Marks): Sender's Addr (½), Date (½), Receiver's Addr (½), Salutation (½), Subject (½), Subscription (½). Deduct marks for incorrect layout.
- Content (4 Marks): Clear articulation of points, relevance, and logical sequencing.
- Expression (3 Marks): Grammatical accuracy, vocabulary, tone (formal and polite).

SECTION B: LITERATURE (25 Marks)
Q5. Extract Based Questions (Act 3, Antony's Speech):
"Friends, Romans, countrymen, lend me your ears..."
Answer: 10 Marks
(i) Setting & Speaker: The scene takes place in the Roman Forum (marketplace). Brutus spoke to the crowd just before Antony, justifying Caesar's assassination as an act for the good of Rome. [2 Marks]

(ii) Antony's Motive: Antony claims he is only there to bury his friend to pacify the hostile, pro-Brutus crowd initially. However, his actual motive is to subtly incite a rebellion/mutiny against the conspirators and avenge Caesar's death. [2 Marks]

(iii) Manipulation of "Honourable": Antony repeatedly refers to Brutus and the conspirators as "honourable men" with heavy irony and sarcasm. Each time he uses the word, he immediately contrasts it with evidence of Caesar's virtues (his military victories, weeping with the poor, refusing the crown). This psychological manipulation gradually makes the crowd realize that the "honourable" men are actually cold-blooded murderers. [3 Marks]

(iv) The Document: Antony produces Caesar's will. He reads that Caesar has left to every Roman citizen seventy-five drachmas and has made his private arbours and newly planted orchards on this side of the Tiber public parks for the citizens to enjoy. [3 Marks]
Marking Scheme: Deduct marks for factual inaccuracies regarding the plot. Spelling of characters/locations (Forum, Brutus, Drachmas) should be largely correct.
Q6. Extract Based Questions (Act 2, Caesar's bravery):
"Cowards die many times before their deaths..."
Answer: 10 Marks
(i) Context: These words are spoken to his wife, Calpurnia. She had a terrifying nightmare about Caesar's statue spouting blood and heard reports of horrible omens (lioness whelping in streets, graves opening), prompting her to beg Caesar to stay home. [2 Marks]

(ii) Meaning of Quote: Caesar means that cowards suffer the agony and fear of death continuously because they are always afraid. Brave people, on the other hand, face their fears and only experience physical death once, when it inevitably comes. [2 Marks]

(iii) Decius Brutus's Deception: Decius Brutus arrives and convinces Caesar to go. He flatteringly misinterprets Calpurnia's dream, saying the spouting blood signifies that great Rome shall suck reviving blood from Caesar (a sign of prosperity). He also tempts Caesar by mentioning the Senate plans to offer him a crown that day, and if he stays home, they might change their minds. [3 Marks]

(iv) Tragic Irony: The tragic irony lies in Caesar's immense hubris (pride). He boldly dismisses death and ignores concrete warnings (the Soothsayer's "Beware the Ides of March," Calpurnia's dream, the beast without a heart). His desire to appear fearless and immortal leads him directly into the hands of the conspirators who slaughter him. [3 Marks]
Marking Scheme: Look for exact details: Decius Brutus (not just Brutus), the specific re-interpretation of the dream, and the Senate's crown offer.
Q7. Long Answer Question (Act 4 - Quarrel Scene):
Causes of conflict between Brutus and Cassius and what it reveals about their characters.
Value Points: 5 Marks
Reasons for Conflict:
  • Brutus accuses Cassius of having an "itching palm" (being greedy/corrupt) and selling offices for gold.
  • Brutus had condemned Lucius Pella for taking bribes, ignoring Cassius's letters requesting leniency for him.
  • Brutus is angry because he asked Cassius for money to pay his legions, and he believes Cassius denied him.
Character Revelations:
  • Brutus: Revealed as highly idealistic, rigid, and hypocritical (he condemns extortion but asks Cassius for the very money gained through it). He is deeply affected by the moral weight of Caesar's assassination.
  • Cassius: Revealed as pragmatic, emotional, and fiercely loyal to Brutus despite the insults. He is willing to bend moral rules to win the war, showing a practical nature compared to Brutus's philosophical rigidity.
Marking Scheme (5 Marks):
- Content & Factual Accuracy (3 Marks): Must mention the 'itching palm', Lucius Pella, and the denial of gold.
- Analysis & Expression (2 Marks): Clear contrast between Brutus's idealism and Cassius's pragmatism.

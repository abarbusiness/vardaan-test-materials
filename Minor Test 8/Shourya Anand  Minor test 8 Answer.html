Prehistory vs History
Prehistory: The period of human development when humans did not know how to read and write. There are no written records for this period (e.g., Stone Age).
History: The period for which written records are available. It is based on written facts and evidence.

Categories of Computers
  • Microcomputers: Smallest, least expensive. Designed for individual use. Examples: Desktop PCs, Laptops, Tablets.
  • Minicomputers: Larger, faster, and more expensive than microcomputers. Used in medium-sized organizations where multiple users need access to the same data. Examples: PDP-8, VAX.
  • Mainframe Computers: Very large, fast, and powerful. Can process huge amounts of data and support thousands of users simultaneously. Used in banks, airlines, and government agencies. Examples: IBM Z-series.
  • Supercomputers: The largest, fastest, and most expensive computers in the world. Used for extremely complex calculations like weather forecasting, quantum mechanics, and space research. Examples: PARAM (India), Fugaku.

Ans 12.
Archaeological Sources
Archaeological sources are physical remains of the past. Their types and significance include:
  • Monuments: Old buildings, forts, temples, and palaces. Significance: They reveal the architectural style, engineering skills, and religious beliefs of the period (e.g., Sanchi Stupa).
  • Inscriptions (Epigraphy): Writings engraved on hard surfaces like rocks, pillars, copper plates, and temple walls. Significance: They are highly reliable as they cannot be easily altered. They provide dates, names of kings, and royal orders (e.g., Ashoka's pillars).
  • Artifacts: Man-made objects like tools, pottery, and jewelry. Significance: They show the technological progress and daily lifestyle of ordinary people.
  • Coins (Numismatics): Made of various metals. Significance: Reveal economic conditions, extent of trade, and chronological timelines of kings.
Overall Significance: They are unbiased, direct evidence of the past, crucial for verifying literary sources or reconstructing history where no written records exist (like the Harappan civilization).
[4 Marks for detailing 4 types of sources + 1 Mark for overall significance = 5 Marks]
Ans 13.
Evolution of Computer Languages
Evolution:
  • First Generation (Machine Language): The lowest level, consisting only of binary numbers (0s and 1s). Very difficult to write and debug. Highly hardware-dependent.
  • Second Generation (Assembly Language): Replaced binary with English-like mnemonic codes (e.g., ADD, JMP). Easier than machine language but still hardware-dependent. Required an Assembler to translate.
  • Third Generation (High-Level Language): Uses simple English words and standard mathematical symbols (e.g., C, Java, BASIC). Completely hardware-independent.
Advantages of High-Level Languages:
  • User-Friendly: Easier to learn, read, write, and understand as they use English words.
  • Portability: Programs written on one computer can run on any other computer (machine-independent).
  • Easier Debugging: Locating and fixing errors is much simpler compared to low-level languages.
[3 Marks for the evolution steps + 2 Marks for advantages of HLL = 5 Marks]
Ans 14.
Literary Sources and Challenges
Importance of Literary Sources: Written records (manuscripts) are invaluable as they provide detailed, descriptive accounts of political events, social structures, economic policies, and cultural practices of the past. They bring history to life with stories, biographies, and administrative manuals.

Challenges faced by Historians:
  • Decay of Materials: Ancient manuscripts were written on fragile materials like palm leaves, birch bark, and animal skins, which decay over time or get destroyed by insects.
  • Copying Errors: Before printing presses, scribes copied texts by hand. They often made unintentional errors or deliberately changed words, altering the original meaning over centuries.
  • Evolution of Scripts/Language: Ancient languages and scripts change over time, making older texts very difficult to decipher today.
  • Author's Bias: Many texts (like Prashastis) were written by court poets to praise their kings. They often exaggerated achievements and ignored failures, meaning historians cannot take them as literal truth.
[2 Marks for Importance + 3 Marks for outlining at least 3 distinct challenges = 5 Marks]
Ans 15.
Translators (Language Processors)
(a) Language Processor and its Necessity:
A language processor (or translator) is a system software that converts programming code written by a human (source code) into machine language (object code).
Necessity: A computer's hardware only understands binary language (electrical signals represented as 0s and 1s). Since humans write programs in Assembly or High-Level Languages (using English words), a translator is mandatory to act as a bridge and convert the instructions into a format the hardware can execute.

(b) Assembler's Specific Role:
An Assembler is a highly specific translator that only converts Assembly Language (Second Generation) into machine language.
Difference: While Compilers and Interpreters translate High-Level Languages (Third Generation and above) which are machine-independent, an Assembler translates mnemonic codes (like ADD, SUB) which are hardware-dependent. An Assembler is tied directly to the specific architecture of a computer processor.
[Part A: 1 Mark for definition + 1 Mark for necessity = 2 Marks. Part B: 1 Mark for Assembler definition + 2 Marks for showing the difference = 3 Marks]

6.
Principles of separation.
Ans: [1 Mark each = 3 Marks]
(a) Separating Funnel: Works on the principle of the difference in densities of two immiscible liquids. The heavier liquid forms the lower layer and is drained out first.
(b) Fractional Distillation: Works on the principle of differences in the boiling points of miscible liquids. Components with lower boiling points vaporize and condense first.
(c) Magnetic Separation: Works on the principle of difference in magnetic properties. Magnetic components (iron) are attracted to the magnet, leaving non-magnetic sulfur powder behind.
7.
Exothermic or Endothermic changes.
Ans: [1 Mark each = 3 Marks]
(a) Endothermic Change: Dissolving Ammonium Chloride absorbs thermal energy from the solvent (water), causing the overall temperature to drop (feeling cold).
(b) Exothermic Change: The chemical reaction of Magnesium burning in Oxygen releases a substantial amount of heat energy and brilliant white light.
(c) Endothermic Change: Boiling is a physical change of state requiring continuous absorption of external thermal energy (heat input) to convert liquid water into steam.
8.
Density and Relative Density.
Ans (a): Density ($\rho$) is defined as mass per unit volume of a substance. $$\text{Density} = \frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Volume}}$$ SI unit is $\text{kg/m}^3$. [1.5 Marks]

Ans (b): Relative Density is defined as the ratio of the density of a substance to the density of water at $4^\circ\text{C}$. $$\text{Relative Density} = \frac{\text{Density of Substance}}{\text{Density of Water}}$$ Since it is a ratio of two identical physical quantities (densities), the units cancel out completely. Hence, relative density is a pure number and possesses no unit. [1.5 Marks]
SECTION C (20 Marks)
9.
Physical vs. Chemical changes in a burning candle.
Ans (a): During the burning of a candle, both changes happen simultaneously:
1. Physical Change: The heat of the flame melts the solid wax into liquid wax. This liquid wax flows down and cools to solidify back into wax. No new substance is formed here. [1.5 Marks]
2. Chemical Change: A portion of the molten wax rises up the wick due to capillary action, vaporizes, and burns in the presence of oxygen. This forms carbon dioxide gas and water vapor, accompanied by heat and light. This is permanent and irreversible. [1.5 Marks]

Ans (b):
Products: Carbon Dioxide and Water Vapor. [1 Mark]
Word Equation: $$\text{Wax (Hydrocarbon)} + \text{Oxygen} \xrightarrow{\text{ignition}} \text{Carbon Dioxide} + \text{Water Vapor} + \text{Heat} + \text{Light}$$ [1 Mark]
10.
Separation of Sand, Common Salt, and Ammonium Chloride.
Ans: The separation of this mixture is performed in three consecutive steps: [1 Mark for each step, 2 Marks for overall clarity = 5 Marks]

Step 1: Sublimation (To isolate Ammonium Chloride)
• Place the solid mixture in a porcelain evaporating dish.
• Cover it with an inverted glass funnel, plugging the stem of the funnel with cotton.
• Heat the dish. Ammonium Chloride is sublimable and directly converts to gaseous state. It hits the cool walls of the funnel and deposits back as pure solid ammonium chloride.
• Leftover Residue in dish: Sand and Common Salt.

Step 2: Dissolution and Filtration (To isolate Sand)
• Add distilled water to the remaining residue in the dish and stir well.
• Common Salt dissolves completely, while Sand remains insoluble.
• Filter the mixture using filter paper and a funnel.
• The insoluble Sand is collected as the residue on the filter paper. Wash it with water and dry it.
• Collected filtrate: Salt solution.

Step 3: Evaporation / Crystallization (To isolate Common Salt)
• Heat the salt filtrate in an evaporating dish.
• Water vaporizes entirely into the atmosphere, leaving behind dry, pure crystals of Common Salt (Sodium Chloride) in the dish.
11.
Measurement and Density Bottle calculation.
Ans (a):
Mass of empty bottle ($M_1$) = $25\text{ g}$
Mass of bottle + Water ($M_2$) = $75\text{ g}$
Mass of water ($M_w$) = $M_2 - M_1 = 75\text{ g} - 25\text{ g} = 50\text{ g}$ [0.5 Marks]
Since the density of water is $1\text{ g/cm}^3$: $$\text{Volume of Water} = \frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Density}} = \frac{50\text{ g}}{1\text{ g/cm}^3} = 50\text{ cm}^3$$ Therefore, the volume capacity of the bottle is $50\text{ cm}^3$. [1 Mark]

Ans (b):
Mass of bottle + Liquid 'Y' ($M_3$) = $65\text{ g}$
Mass of liquid 'Y' ($M_y$) = $M_3 - M_1 = 65\text{ g} - 25\text{ g} = 40\text{ g}$ [0.5 Marks]
Since liquid 'Y' completely fills the bottle, its volume ($V$) = $50\text{ cm}^3$. $$\text{Density of Y in g/cm}^3 = \frac{\text{Mass of Y}}{\text{Volume}} = \frac{40\text{ g}}{50\text{ cm}^3} = \mathbf{0.8\text{ g/cm}^3}$$ [0.5 Marks]
Density in SI units: $$\text{Density in kg/m}^3 = 0.8 \times 1000 = \mathbf{800\text{ kg/m}^3}$$ [1 Mark]

Ans (c): $$\text{Relative Density of Y} = \frac{\text{Density of Y in g/cm}^3}{\text{Density of Water in g/cm}^3} = \frac{0.8}{1} = \mathbf{0.8}$$ [0.5 Marks]

Ans (d):
Mass of Liquid 'Y' occupying $250\text{ cm}^3$: $$\text{Mass} = \text{Density} \times \text{Volume}$$ $$\text{Mass} = 0.8\text{ g/cm}^3 \times 250\text{ cm}^3 = \mathbf{200\text{ g}}$$ [1 Mark]
12.
Factors influencing chemical changes and rusting vs. burning of iron.
Ans (a): Three conditions (other than heat) are: [1 Mark for each factor + example = Total 3 Marks]
1. Close Contact: Reactants must be in close contact or dissolved in water (solution form) for a reaction to occur.
• Example: Solid sodium bicarbonate and citric acid do not react, but start fizzing when water is added.
2. Light (Photochemical Reactions): Some reactions require light energy to trigger chemical bonds.
• Example: Photosynthesis ($6CO_2 + 6H_2O \xrightarrow{\text{light}} C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2$) or decomposition of silver nitrate in sunlight.
3. Presence of a Catalyst: A catalyst increases or alters the speed of a chemical reaction without undergoing permanent change.
• Example: Manganese dioxide ($MnO_2$) accelerates the decomposition of Hydrogen Peroxide ($H_2O_2$) to produce Oxygen gas.

Ans (b): Differences between Rusting and Burning of Iron: [1 Mark each = Total 2 Marks]
Rusting of Iron Burning of Iron
It is a slow, gradual oxidation process taking days or weeks. It is a rapid, instantaneous oxidation process when heated in flame.
Occurs at room temperature in the presence of moisture (water) and oxygen. Occurs at high temperature on heating and does not require moisture.
Produces brown, flaky Hydrated Iron Oxide ($Fe_2O_3 \cdot xH_2O$). Produces dark magnetic Iron Oxide ($Fe_3O_4$) with release of light energy.

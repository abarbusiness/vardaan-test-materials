MARKING SCHEME / ANSWER KEY - Minor Test 8
Class: 6 ICSE Subject: Science (Physics & Chemistry) Time: 1 Hour Max. Marks: 50
SECTION A (10 Marks)
1.
Define 'Matter'. State its two fundamental characteristics.
Ans: Anything that occupies space and has mass is called matter. [1 Mark]
Characteristics: (i) It has mass. (ii) It occupies space (has volume). [1 Mark - 0.5 each]
2.
What is meant by the 'atomicity' of an element? Give one example of a diatomic element.
Ans: The number of atoms present in one molecule of an element is called its atomicity. [1 Mark]
Example: Oxygen (O2), Nitrogen (N2), or Hydrogen (H2). [1 Mark for any one correct example]
3.
Differentiate between evaporation and boiling.
Ans:
  • Evaporation: It takes place at all temperatures. It is a slow process and a surface phenomenon.
  • Boiling: It takes place at a fixed temperature (boiling point). It is a rapid process and a bulk phenomenon.
[1 Mark for each valid pair of differences = Total 2 Marks]
4.
Write the correct chemical symbols...
Ans: (a) Sodium: Na [0.5 Marks]    (b) Iron: Fe [0.5 Marks]    (c) Potassium: K [0.5 Marks]    (d) Gold: Au [0.5 Marks]
5.
Why are gases highly compressible while solids are almost incompressible?
Ans: Gases have extremely large intermolecular spaces, so their molecules can be easily pushed closer together under pressure. [1 Mark]
Solids have tightly packed molecules with negligible intermolecular space, leaving no room for further compression. [1 Mark]
SECTION B (15 Marks)
6.
Explain the process of 'Sublimation'. Name any two substances that undergo sublimation.
Ans: The process in which a solid changes directly into its gaseous state on heating, without passing through the liquid state, is called sublimation. [1.5 Marks]
Examples: Camphor, Naphthalene balls, Iodine, Dry Ice. [1.5 Marks - 0.75 for each correct example]
7.
Classify into elements and compounds...
Ans:
Elements: Oxygen, Iron, Nitrogen [1.5 Marks - 0.5 each]
Compounds: Water, Carbon dioxide, Calcium carbonate [1.5 Marks - 0.5 each]
8.
Define 'Intermolecular force of attraction'. How does magnitude vary?
Ans: The force of attraction existing between the particles (molecules) of matter is called the intermolecular force of attraction. [1 Mark]
Variation: It is maximum (strongest) in solids, intermediate (weaker than solids) in liquids, and negligible (minimum) in gases. [2 Marks]
9.
Write the chemical formulae...
Ans:
(a) Water: H2O [1 Mark]
(b) Carbon dioxide: CO2 [1 Mark]
(c) Sodium chloride: NaCl [1 Mark]
10.
State any three key differences between an element and a compound.
Ans: [1 Mark for each correct point = 3 Marks]
Element Compound
Made up of only one kind of atoms. Made up of two or more different kinds of atoms chemically combined.
Cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical methods. Can be broken down into simpler substances (its constituent elements) by chemical methods.
Has its own unique set of physical and chemical properties. Properties are entirely different from the properties of its constituent elements.
SECTION C (25 Marks)
11.
(a) Molecular arrangement in solid, liquid, gas. (b) Reason for definite shape.
Ans (a):
- Solids: Molecules are very closely packed. Intermolecular space is negligible. [1 Mark]
- Liquids: Molecules are loosely packed compared to solids. Intermolecular space is larger than in solids. [1 Mark]
- Gases: Molecules are very far apart. Intermolecular space is very large. [1 Mark]
Ans (b): Solids have strong intermolecular forces that hold the molecules tightly in fixed positions, giving them a definite shape. Liquids have weaker intermolecular forces, allowing molecules to slip and slide over one another, hence they take the shape of the container. [2 Marks]
12.
Chemical formula information and specific compound breakdowns.
Ans: A chemical formula indicates the types of elements present in a compound and the exact number of atoms of each element in one molecule of that substance. [Evaluator Note: Checked inherently within the sub-parts]
(a) Sulphuric acid (H2SO4):
Elements: Hydrogen (2 atoms), Sulphur (1 atom), Oxygen (4 atoms). [1.5 Marks]
Total number of atoms = 2 + 1 + 4 = 7 atoms. [1 Mark]
(b) Glucose (C6H12O6):
Elements: Carbon (6 atoms), Hydrogen (12 atoms), Oxygen (6 atoms). [1.5 Marks]
Total number of atoms = 6 + 12 + 6 = 24 atoms. [1 Mark]
13.
Inter-conversion of matter and definitions.
Ans: The phenomenon of change of one state of matter into another, and then back to the original state (by altering temperature/pressure) is called inter-conversion of states of matter. [1 Mark]
- Melting: The process of conversion of a solid into a liquid on heating. [1 Mark]
- Freezing: The process of conversion of a liquid into a solid on cooling. [1 Mark]
- Vaporization (Boiling): The process of conversion of a liquid into a gas on heating. [1 Mark]
- Condensation: The process of conversion of a gas into a liquid on cooling. [1 Mark]
14.
(a) Pure substance. (b) Refuting "Air is a compound".
Ans (a): A pure substance is made up of only one kind of particles (atoms or molecules) and has a fixed, uniform chemical composition. [1 Mark]
Ans (b): Air is a mixture, not a compound, because: [1 Mark each = 4 Marks]
  1. The constituents of air (oxygen, nitrogen, etc.) retain their individual chemical properties.
  2. The components can be separated by simple physical methods (e.g., fractional distillation of liquid air).
  3. The composition of air is not fixed; the proportion of gases can vary from place to place.
  4. No chemical reaction takes place and no heat/light energy is exchanged when gases mix to form air.
15.
Scenario based on substance 'X'.
Ans:
(a) State of matter: Liquid state (Liquids have no fixed shape but fixed volume). [1 Mark]
(b) Physical process: Vaporization / Boiling / Evaporation. [1 Mark]
(c) Properties of Metals: Metals are generally hard, lustrous (shiny), malleable (can be beaten into sheets), ductile (can be drawn into wires), and are good conductors of heat and electricity. [2 Marks for any 3 properties]
Example: Iron, Copper, Aluminium, Gold (Any one). [1 Mark]

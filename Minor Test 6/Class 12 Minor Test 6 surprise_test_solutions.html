Question 1
Define the term 'order of a reaction'. How does it differ from the molecularity of a reaction? Give one point of difference.
Answer:
Order: The sum of powers of the concentration of the reactants in the rate law expression is called the order of that chemical reaction. (1)
Difference: Order can be zero or a fraction, whereas molecularity can never be zero or a fraction; it is always a whole number. (1)
Question 2
A reaction is first order with respect to reactant A and second order with respect to reactant B. Write the differential rate equation for this reaction.
Answer:
Differential Rate equation:
Rate = -d[R]/dt = k[A]1[B]2 (2)
Question 3
Identify the reaction order from the following rate constant: k = 2.3 × 10-5 L mol-1 s-1.
Answer:
The unit of rate constant is L mol-1 s-1 (or mol-1 L s-1).
General unit is (mol L-1)1-n s-1.
Comparing, 1 - n = -1 ⇒ n = 2.
Therefore, it is a Second-order reaction. (2)
Question 4
Write the integrated rate equations for a zero-order and a first-order reaction. Also, write down the unit of the rate constant for a second-order reaction and state the Arrhenius equation.
Answer:
Zero-order integrated equation: [R] = [R]0 - kt (0.5)
First-order integrated equation: k = (2.303/t) log([R]0/[R]) (0.5)
Second-order rate constant unit: L mol-1 s-1 (0.5)
Arrhenius equation: k = A e-Ea/RT (0.5)
Question 5
What is the effect of adding a positive catalyst on the activation energy (Ea) of a chemical reaction?
Answer:
A positive catalyst provides an alternate path or mechanism for the reaction which has a lower activation energy. Thus, it decreases the activation energy of the reaction. (2)
SECTION B (3 Marks Each)
Question 6
For a first-order reaction, show that the time required for 99% completion is approximately twice the time required for the completion of 90% of the reaction.
Answer:
For a 1st order reaction: t = (2.303 / k) log (a / (a-x))
For 99% completion: x = 0.99a
t99% = (2.303 / k) log (a / 0.01a) = (2.303 / k) log 100 = (2.303 / k) × 2 (1.5)
For 90% completion: x = 0.90a
t90% = (2.303 / k) log (a / 0.10a) = (2.303 / k) log 10 = (2.303 / k) × 1 (1)
Comparing both equations: t99% = 2 × t90%. (Hence Proved) (0.5)
Question 7
The rate constant for a first-order reaction is 60 s-1. How much time will it take to reduce the initial concentration of the reactant to its 1/16th value?
Answer:
Formula: t = (2.303 / k) log ([R]0 / [R])
Given k = 60 s-1, [R] = [R]0 / 16 (1)
t = (2.303 / 60) log (16)
t = (2.303 / 60) × 1.2041 (1)
t ≈ 0.0462 s (or 4.62 × 10-2 s) (1)
Question 8
Differentiate between the Average rate and the Instantaneous rate of a reaction. How is the instantaneous rate represented mathematically?
Answer:
Average Rate: Change in concentration of reactants or products divided by the macroscopic time interval (Δt) over which the change occurs.
Mathematical representation: ravg = -Δ[R]/Δt = +Δ[P]/Δt (1.5)
Instantaneous Rate: Rate of reaction at a particular exact moment of time (when Δt approaches zero).
Mathematical representation: rinst = -d[R]/dt = +d[P]/dt (1.5)
Question 9
Explain the following terms briefly:
(i) Activation Energy (Ea)
(ii) Threshold Energy
Answer:
(i) Activation Energy: The minimum extra amount of energy absorbed by the reactant molecules so that their energy becomes equal to the threshold value. (1.5)
(ii) Threshold Energy: The minimum total kinetic energy that the reacting molecules must have in order to undergo an effective collision leading to product formation. (1.5)
Question 10
A first-order reaction has a rate constant of 1.15 × 10-3 s-1. How long will 5 g of this reactant take to reduce to 3 g?
(Given: log 5 = 0.6990, log 3 = 0.4771)
Answer:
t = (2.303 / k) log ([R]0 / [R]) (1)
t = [2.303 / (1.15 × 10-3)] × log (5 / 3)
t = 2002.6 × (log 5 - log 3)
t = 2002.6 × (0.6990 - 0.4771) = 2002.6 × 0.2219 (1)
t ≈ 444.38 s (1)

Question 11
(a) Derive the integrated rate equation for a first-order reaction: R → Products. (3)
(b) Plot a graph between ln[R] versus time (t) for a first-order reaction and state what the slope of this line represents. (2)
Answer:
(a) Derivation:
R → P
Rate = -d[R]/dt = k[R] ⇒ -d[R]/[R] = k dt (1)
Integrating both sides: ∫ -d[R]/[R] = k ∫ dt ⇒ -ln[R] = kt + I (where I is integration constant) (1)
At t = 0, [R] = [R]0, so I = -ln[R]0.
Substituting I back: -ln[R] = kt - ln[R]0 ⇒ kt = ln([R]0/[R]) or k = (2.303/t)log([R]0/[R]) (1)
(b) Graph:
Equation: ln[R] = -kt + ln[R]0 (y = mx + c)
Graph is a straight line sloping downwards. (1 for plotting correct axes and line)
Slope = -k (1)
Question 12
The temperature dependence of the rate of a chemical reaction is given by the Arrhenius equation.
(a) Write the Arrhenius equation and define all the terms involved in it. (2)
(b) The rate of a reaction quadruples (becomes four times) when the temperature changes from 293 K to 313 K. Calculate the energy of activation (Ea) of the reaction assuming that it does not change with temperature.
(Given R = 8.314 J K-1 mol-1, log 4 = 0.6021) (3)
Answer:
(a) Arrhenius Equation: k = A e-Ea/RT (1)
where: k = rate constant, A = Arrhenius factor / Frequency factor, Ea = Activation energy, R = Gas constant, T = Absolute Temperature. (1)
(b) Numerical:
Formula: log (k2/k1) = [Ea / 2.303R] × [(T2 - T1) / (T1T2)] (1)
Given k2 = 4k1, so log 4 = 0.6021. T1 = 293 K, T2 = 313 K.
0.6021 = [Ea / (2.303 × 8.314)] × [(313 - 293) / (293 × 313)] (1)
0.6021 = (Ea / 19.147) × (20 / 91709)
Ea = (0.6021 × 19.147 × 91709) / 20 = 52863 J/mol = 52.86 kJ/mol (1)

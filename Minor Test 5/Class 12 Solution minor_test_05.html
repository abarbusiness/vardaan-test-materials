Q1. A standard Daniel cell has a potential of 1.1 V. Predict the direction of flow of electrons and the chemical reaction taking place if an opposing external potential of (i) exactly 1.1 V and (ii) 1.2 V is applied to this cell.
Key Concept (Reversible Cells): An electrochemical cell acts as a galvanic cell (generates electricity) as long as the external opposing potential (Eext) is less than the cell potential (Ecell).
  • If Eext = Ecell: The cell reaction halts. No current flows.
  • If Eext > Ecell: The cell acts as an electrolytic cell. The reaction reverses, and non-spontaneous chemistry occurs driven by external electricity.
Solution:
(i) When Eext = 1.1 V:
Because the external potential perfectly balances the cell potential (1.1 V), the system reaches a state of equilibrium.
• Flow of electrons: Stops entirely. No current flows.
• Chemical reaction: No chemical reaction occurs.
(ii) When Eext = 1.2 V:
The external potential is greater than the cell potential (1.2 V > 1.1 V). The cell is forced to behave as an electrolytic cell. The standard Daniel cell reaction (Zn + Cu2+ → Zn2+ + Cu) reverses.
• Flow of electrons: Electrons will now flow in the reverse direction, from Copper (Cu) to Zinc (Zn) in the external circuit. Current flows from Zn to Cu.
• Chemical reaction: Zn is deposited at the zinc electrode, and Cu dissolves at the copper electrode. Reaction: Cu(s) + Zn2+(aq) → Cu2+(aq) + Zn(s).
Q2. Explain, using interionic interactions, why the conductivity (κ) of an electrolytic solution decreases with dilution, whereas its molar conductivity (Λm) increases with dilution.
Key Concept (Conductivity vs Molar Conductivity):
• Conductivity (κ): Conductance of 1 cm3 of solution. It strictly depends on the number of ions per unit volume.
• Molar Conductivity (Λm): Conducting power of all ions produced by dissolving 1 mole of electrolyte.
Λm = (κ × 1000) / Molarity
Solution:
1. Why Conductivity (κ) decreases: When we dilute a solution, we are adding more solvent (water). Although the total number of ions might increase slightly (for weak electrolytes), the volume of the solution increases at a much faster rate. Consequently, the number of current-carrying ions present in exactly 1 cm3 of the solution strictly decreases. Hence, κ decreases.

2. Why Molar Conductivity (Λm) increases: Based on the formula, Λm is the product of κ and Volume (V = 1000/M). Upon dilution, the decrease in κ is overcompensated by the massive increase in Volume (V).
Interionic explanation: In concentrated solutions, oppositely charged ions are close to each other, causing strong electrostatic attractions (interionic friction) which slow down their movement. Upon dilution, ions move further apart. This decreases the interionic attractions drastically, increasing ionic mobility, and thus increasing Λm.
Q3. During the electrolysis of aqueous sodium chloride, sodium ions are not reduced at the cathode, but hydrogen gas is evolved. However, chlorine gas is preferentially liberated at the anode despite oxygen having a lower oxidation potential. Give rigorous thermodynamic or kinetic reasons for these two observations.
Key Concept (Preferential Discharge & Overpotential): When multiple ions compete at an electrode, the one requiring the least energy (most favorable standard potential) is discharged first. However, some gases (like O2) require extra kinetic energy (Overpotential) to bubble out, altering the thermodynamic expectations.
Solution:
At the Cathode (Reduction):
Two competing reactions:
1. Na+(aq) + e- → Na(s) [E° = -2.71 V]
2. H2O(l) + e- → ½H2(g) + OH-(aq) [E° = -0.83 V]
Reason: The reduction potential of water (-0.83 V) is much higher (less negative) than that of sodium (-2.71 V). Thermodynamically, it is much easier to reduce water. Therefore, H2 gas is evolved.

At the Anode (Oxidation):
Two competing reactions:
1. 2Cl-(aq) → Cl2(g) + 2e- [E°ox = -1.36 V]
2. 2H2O(l) → O2(g) + 4H+(aq) + 4e- [E°ox = -1.23 V]
Reason: Thermodynamically, water has a higher (less negative) oxidation potential (-1.23 V) compared to chloride (-1.36 V), so O2 should evolve. However, the kinetic formation of O2 gas on standard electrodes is very slow and requires an extra voltage push, known as kinetic overpotential (or overvoltage). Because of this high overpotential for O2, the oxidation of Cl- becomes kinetically more favorable. Hence, Cl2 gas is liberated.
Q4. Consider the standard electrode potentials: E°A2+/A = -2.37 V, E°B2+/B = -0.76 V, E°C2+/C = 0.34 V, and E°D+/D = 0.80 V. Arrange these metals in increasing order of their reducing power and identify the strongest oxidizing agent among the ions. Justify your answer.
Key Concept (Electrochemical Series):
• The lower (more negative) the Standard Reduction Potential (SRP), the easier it is for the metal to lose electrons. Hence, More Negative SRP = Stronger Reducing Agent.
• The higher (more positive) the SRP, the easier it is for the ion to gain electrons. Hence, More Positive SRP = Stronger Oxidizing Agent.
Solution:
Given SRPs: A = -2.37 V, B = -0.76 V, C = 0.34 V, D = 0.80 V.

1. Increasing order of Reducing Power:
Reducing power increases as SRP becomes more negative.
Order of SRP: D (+0.80 V) > C (+0.34 V) > B (-0.76 V) > A (-2.37 V).
Therefore, reducing power order: D < C < B < A.

2. Strongest Oxidizing Agent:
The species that undergoes reduction most readily is the strongest oxidizing agent. This corresponds to the highest positive SRP.
Among the given ions, D+ has the highest SRP (+0.80 V).
Therefore, D+ is the strongest oxidizing agent.
Q5. How much charge is required for the complete reduction of 1 mole of MnO4- to Mn2+ in an acidic medium? Express your answer rigorously in Coulombs.
Key Concept (Faraday's Laws): The amount of charge required depends on the number of moles of electrons (n) exchanged in the balanced half-reaction. 1 mole of electrons carries 1 Faraday of charge.
Charge (Q) = n × F
Where F (Faraday's Constant) ≈ 96500 Coulombs (C)
Solution:
Step 1: Write the balanced half-reaction for the reduction in acidic medium to find 'n'.
MnO4- + 8H+ + 5e- → Mn2+ + 4H2O

Step 2: Determine oxidation states to verify.
Oxidation state of Mn in MnO4- is +7.
Oxidation state of Mn in Mn2+ is +2.
Change in oxidation state = (+7) - (+2) = 5. Therefore, n = 5 moles of electrons per mole of MnO4-.

Step 3: Calculate Charge (Q).
Q = n × F = 5 × 96500 C
Total Charge Required = 482,500 Coulombs (or 4.825 × 105 C)
SECTION B (15 Marks) - Short Answer Type
Q6. Calculate the electrode potential of a standard hydrogen electrode placed in a buffer solution with a pH of 10 at 298 K. (Given: H2 gas is at 1 atm pressure).
Key Concept (Nernst Equation for SHE): The Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE) has a standard potential E° = 0.00 V. The reaction is: 2H+(aq) + 2e- → H2(g). The Nernst equation allows calculation at non-standard conditions.
E = E° - (0.0591 / n) × log10( PH2 / [H+]2 )
Simplified for Hydrogen: E = -0.0591 × pH
Solution:
Given: pH = 10.
We know that pH = -log10[H+]
Therefore, [H+] = 10-10 M.
Pressure of H2 gas (PH2) = 1 atm.

Applying the Nernst equation for the reduction half-reaction: 2H+ + 2e- → H2
EH+/H2 = E°H+/H2 - (0.0591 / 2) × log( PH2 / [H+]2 )
EH+/H2 = 0.00 - 0.02955 × log( 1 / (10-10)2 )
EH+/H2 = - 0.02955 × log( 1020 )
EH+/H2 = - 0.02955 × 20 = -0.591 V.

Alternative shortcut method: E = -0.0591 × pH = -0.0591 × 10 = -0.591 V.
Electrode Potential (E) = -0.591 V
Q7. The resistance of a conductivity cell filled with 0.1 M KCl solution is 100 Ω. If the resistance of the same cell when filled with 0.02 M KCl solution is 520 Ω, calculate the conductivity and molar conductivity of the 0.02 M KCl solution. (Given: The conductivity of 0.1 M KCl solution is 1.29 S m-1).
Key Concept (Cell Constant): The physical dimensions of the conductivity cell (distance between electrodes, area) remain constant. This ratio is the Cell Constant (G*). Once calculated for a known solution, it can be applied to any unknown solution in the same cell.
Cell Constant (G*) = Conductivity (κ) × Resistance (R)
κunknown = G* / Runknown
Λm = (κ × 1000) / Molarity (Ensure units are aligned!)
Solution:
Step 1: Calculate the Cell Constant (G*) using 0.1 M KCl data.
Given: R1 = 100 Ω, κ1 = 1.29 S m-1
G* = κ1 × R1 = 1.29 S m-1 × 100 Ω = 129 m-1.
(To convert to standard cm-1: 129 m-1 = 1.29 cm-1)

Step 2: Calculate Conductivity (κ2) of 0.02 M KCl.
Given: R2 = 520 Ω, G* = 129 m-1 (or 1.29 cm-1).
Using cm units for standard chemistry calculations: κ2 = G* / R2 = 1.29 cm-1 / 520 Ω
κ2 = 2.48 × 10-3 S cm-1 (or 0.248 S m-1).

Step 3: Calculate Molar Conductivity (Λm) of 0.02 M KCl.
C = 0.02 M (mol L-1)
Using the standard formula with κ in S cm-1:
Λm = (κ2 × 1000) / C
Λm = (2.48 × 10-3 × 1000) / 0.02 = 2.48 / 0.02 = 124.
Conductivity (κ) = 2.48 × 10-3 S cm-1
Molar Conductivity (Λm) = 124 S cm2 mol-1
Q8. State Kohlrausch's law of independent migration of ions. How does this law practically help in determining the limiting molar conductivity (Λ°m) of a weak electrolyte like acetic acid, which cannot be accurately determined by graphical extrapolation?
Key Concept (Kohlrausch's Law): At infinite dilution, interionic forces are zero. Every ion migrates independently, and its contribution to total conductivity is a fixed value, regardless of the other ion present.
Solution:
Statement of Kohlrausch's Law:
The limiting molar conductivity of an electrolyte (at infinite dilution) can be represented as the sum of the individual contributions of the anion and the cation of the electrolyte.
Mathematically: Λ°m = ν+λ°+ + ν-λ°-
(Where λ°+ and λ°- are limiting molar conductivities of cation and anion, and ν represents the number of respective ions).

Application for Weak Electrolytes:
For a weak electrolyte like acetic acid (CH3COOH), the curve of Λm vs √C is very steep near zero concentration, meaning it never touches the y-axis. Thus, graphical extrapolation to find Λ°m is impossible.
Kohlrausch's law allows us to calculate it indirectly using strong electrolytes (which can be graphed). We choose three strong electrolytes that contain the required ions: CH3COONa, HCl, and NaCl.

By the law:
Λ°m(CH3COOH) = λ°(CH3COO-) + λ°(H+)

We algebraically rearrange the values of the strong electrolytes:
Λ°m(CH3COOH) = [λ°(CH3COO-) + λ°(Na+)] + [λ°(H+) + λ°(Cl-)] - [λ°(Na+) + λ°(Cl-)]
Formula: Λ°m(CH3COOH) = Λ°m(CH3COONa) + Λ°m(HCl) - Λ°m(NaCl)
This simple addition and subtraction yields the exact value.
Q9. A concentration cell is constructed by dipping two identical copper electrodes in aqueous CuSO4 solutions of concentrations 0.001 M and 0.1 M respectively at 298 K.
(i) Write the standard cell representation and clearly assign the anode and cathode.
(ii) Calculate the EMF of this cell at 298 K.
Key Concept (Concentration Cells): Both electrodes are of the same material, and solutions are of the same electrolyte but different concentrations. Standard cell potential E° = 0. Current flows simply due to the concentration gradient. Oxidation (Anode) must happen in the less concentrated half-cell to increase its concentration, driving the system towards equilibrium.
Ecell = - (0.0591 / n) × log ( [Canode] / [Ccathode] )
Solution:
(i) Cell Representation:
• Anode (Oxidation): Occurs in the less concentrated solution (0.001 M) to generate more Cu2+ ions.
• Cathode (Reduction): Occurs in the more concentrated solution (0.1 M) to consume Cu2+ ions.
Cell Notation: Cu(s) | Cu2+ (0.001 M) || Cu2+ (0.1 M) | Cu(s)

(ii) Calculation of EMF:
For Copper, n = 2 electrons (Cu → Cu2+ + 2e-).
Because electrodes are identical, E°cell = 0.00 V.
Using Nernst Equation:
Ecell = E°cell - (0.0591 / 2) × log ( [Anode] / [Cathode] )
Ecell = 0 - (0.0591 / 2) × log ( 0.001 / 0.1 )
Ecell = - 0.02955 × log ( 10-3 / 10-1 )
Ecell = - 0.02955 × log ( 10-2 )
Ecell = - 0.02955 × (-2)
Ecell = +0.0591 V.
Cell EMF = 0.0591 V
Q10. Corrosion of iron is essentially an electrochemical phenomenon. Explain this statement by writing the specific anodic and cathodic reactions that occur during the rusting of iron in the presence of water and atmospheric oxygen.
Key Concept (Electrochemical Theory of Rusting): Impure iron surfaces act as miniature galvanic cells in the presence of water (acting as an electrolyte) and dissolved oxygen.
Solution:
According to the electrochemical theory, the uneven surface of iron, coupled with water containing dissolved CO2 (forming carbonic acid, H2CO3), creates tiny electrochemical cells on the metal surface.

1. Anodic Region: Pure iron acts as the anode. Iron atoms lose electrons and go into the solution as ferrous ions.
Anode Reaction: 2Fe(s) → 2Fe2+(aq) + 4e-

2. Cathodic Region: Electrons released at the anode flow through the metal to another spot, which acts as the cathode. Here, atmospheric oxygen is reduced in the presence of H+ ions (from water/carbonic acid).
Cathode Reaction: O2(g) + 4H+(aq) + 4e- → 2H2O(l)

3. Overall Cell Reaction: Adding both half-reactions gives:
2Fe(s) + O2(g) + 4H+(aq) → 2Fe2+(aq) + 2H2O(l)

Subsequent reaction (Rust Formation): The Fe2+ ions are further oxidized by atmospheric oxygen to form Ferric ions (Fe3+), which precipitate as hydrated ferric oxide (Rust).
2Fe2+(aq) + ½O2(g) + 2H2O(l) → Fe2O3(s) + 4H+(aq)
Fe2O3 + xH2O → Fe2O3·xH2O (Rust)

Q11. Three electrolytic cells A, B, and C containing solutions of ZnSO4, AgNO3, and CuSO4 respectively, are connected in series. A steady direct current of 1.5 Amperes was passed through them until exactly 1.45 g of silver was deposited at the cathode of cell B.
(a) State Faraday's First and Second Laws of electrolysis which govern this setup.
(b) Calculate the exact time for which the current flowed in the circuit.
(c) What exact mass of copper and zinc were deposited in cells C and A respectively? (Given atomic masses: Ag = 108 u, Cu = 63.5 u, Zn = 65.3 u, F = 96500 C mol-1).
Key Concept (Faraday's Laws):
Cells in series receive the exact same amount of electrical charge (Q). Faraday's Second Law dictates that the mass deposited in series cells is directly proportional to their equivalent weights.
First Law: m = Z × I × t = (M / nF) × I × t
Second Law: m1 / E1 = m2 / E2
Solution:
(a) Statement of Laws:
• First Law: The amount of chemical reaction (mass of substance deposited or liberated) at any electrode is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity (charge, Q) passed through the electrolyte.
• Second Law: When the same quantity of electricity is passed through different electrolytes connected in series, the masses of the substances produced at the electrodes are directly proportional to their equivalent weights (Atomic Mass / Valency).

(b) Calculation of Time (t):
Focus on Cell B (Silver).
Reaction: Ag+ + 1e- → Ag(s) [n = 1]
Mass of Ag (m) = 1.45 g, Current (I) = 1.5 A, Molar mass (M) = 108 g/mol.
Using First Law: m = (M / nF) × I × t
1.45 = (108 / (1 × 96500)) × 1.5 × t
t = (1.45 × 96500) / (108 × 1.5) = 139925 / 162
t ≈ 863.73 seconds (approx 14 minutes and 24 seconds).

(c) Mass of Copper and Zinc:
Using Faraday's Second Law. Let Equivalent Weight be E = Atomic Mass / n.
EAg = 108 / 1 = 108
ECu = 63.5 / 2 = 31.75
EZn = 65.3 / 2 = 32.65

For Copper (Cell C):
mCu / ECu = mAg / EAg
mCu / 31.75 = 1.45 / 108
mCu = (1.45 × 31.75) / 108 = 46.0375 / 108 ≈ 0.426 g.

For Zinc (Cell A):
mZn / EZn = mAg / EAg
mZn / 32.65 = 1.45 / 108
mZn = (1.45 × 32.65) / 108 = 47.3425 / 108 ≈ 0.438 g.
Time (t) = 863.73 s | Mass of Cu = 0.426 g | Mass of Zn = 0.438 g
Q12. For the reaction occurring in a standard galvanic cell: Mg(s) + 2Ag+(aq) → Mg2+(aq) + 2Ag(s)
(a) Represent the cell schematically.
(b) Calculate the standard cell potential (E°cell) and the standard Gibbs free energy change (ΔG°) at 298 K. (Given: E°Mg2+/Mg = -2.36 V, E°Ag+/Ag = 0.80 V, F = 96500 C mol-1).
(c) Calculate the maximum electrical work that can be strictly obtained from this cell under standard conditions.
(d) Determine the equilibrium constant (Kc) for the reaction at 298 K.
Key Concepts (Thermodynamics of Cells):
• E°cell = E°cathode - E°anode
• ΔG° = -nFE°cell (Gibbs energy is the negative of maximum useful electrical work).
• Equilibrium constant relates to standard potential via: log Kc = (nE°) / 0.0591
Solution:
(a) Cell Representation:
Anode (Oxidation): Mg → Mg2+ + 2e-
Cathode (Reduction): 2Ag+ + 2e- → 2Ag
Representation: Mg(s) | Mg2+(aq) || Ag+(aq) | Ag(s)

(b) Standard Cell Potential & Gibbs Free Energy:
E°cell = E°Cathode - E°Anode
E°cell = E°Ag+/Ag - E°Mg2+/Mg
E°cell = 0.80 V - (-2.36 V) = 3.16 V.

Number of electrons exchanged, n = 2.
ΔG° = -n F E°cell
ΔG° = -2 × 96500 C mol-1 × 3.16 V
ΔG° = -609,880 J mol-1 = -609.88 kJ mol-1.

(c) Maximum Electrical Work:
Maximum electrical work done by a galvanic cell is equal to the decrease in its Gibbs free energy.
Wmax = - ΔG° = -(-609.88 kJ mol-1) = 609.88 kJ mol-1.

(d) Equilibrium Constant (Kc):
Using the formula: E°cell = (0.0591 / n) × log Kc
3.16 = (0.0591 / 2) × log Kc
log Kc = (3.16 × 2) / 0.0591
log Kc = 6.32 / 0.0591 = 106.937...
Kc = Antilog(106.937) = 100.937 × 10106 ≈ 8.65 × 10106.
E° = 3.16 V | ΔG° = -609.88 kJ/mol | Wmax = 609.88 kJ/mol | Kc = 8.65 × 10106
Q13. The conductivity (κ) of a 0.001028 M aqueous solution of acetic acid (CH3COOH) is experimentally found to be 4.95 × 10-5 S cm-1 at 298 K.
(a) Calculate the molar conductivity (Λm) of this specific solution.
(b) If the limiting molar conductivities (Λ°m) of H+ and CH3COO- ions are 349.6 S cm2 mol-1 and 40.9 S cm2 mol-1 respectively, calculate the degree of dissociation (α) of acetic acid at this given concentration.
(c) Calculate the acid dissociation constant (Ka) of the acetic acid.
(d) What mathematically happens to the degree of dissociation (α) if this solution is further diluted with pure water? Justify briefly using Le Chatelier's principle or relevant formulas.
Key Concept (Weak Electrolyte Equilibria):
• Molar Conductivity at concentration C: Λm = (κ × 1000) / C
• Degree of Dissociation: α = Λm / Λ°m (Ratio of conductivity at given C to conductivity at infinite dilution).
• Acid Dissociation Constant (Ostwald's Law): Ka = (C α2) / (1 - α)
Solution:
Given: κ = 4.95 × 10-5 S cm-1, C = 0.001028 M.

(a) Molar Conductivity (Λm):
Λm = (κ × 1000) / C
Λm = (4.95 × 10-5 × 1000) / 0.001028
Λm = 0.0495 / 0.001028 = 48.15 S cm2 mol-1.

(b) Degree of Dissociation (α):
First, find Limiting Molar Conductivity (Λ°m) using Kohlrausch's law:
Λ°m(CH3COOH) = λ°(H+) + λ°(CH3COO-)
Λ°m = 349.6 + 40.9 = 390.5 S cm2 mol-1.
Now calculate α:
α = Λm / Λ°m = 48.15 / 390.5 ≈ 0.1233 (or 12.33%).

(c) Acid Dissociation Constant (Ka):
Reaction: CH3COOH ↔ CH3COO- + H+
Initial C: C ........ 0 ........ 0
Equilib.: C(1-α) .. Cα ....... Cα
Ka = [CH3COO-][H+] / [CH3COOH] = (Cα × Cα) / C(1-α) = Cα2 / (1-α)
Ka = (0.001028 × (0.1233)2) / (1 - 0.1233)
Ka = (0.001028 × 0.01520) / 0.8767
Ka = 0.00001562 / 0.8767 ≈ 1.78 × 10-5.

(d) Effect of Further Dilution:
According to Ostwald's Dilution Law (Ka ≈ Cα2 for weak electrolytes where 1-α ≈ 1), we get α = √(Ka / C) or α = √(Ka × V).
Because Ka is a constant at a given temperature, mathematically, as Concentration (C) decreases (or Volume V increases), the degree of dissociation (α) must increase.
Le Chatelier's Principle: Adding water dilutes all species, decreasing their concentrations. The equilibrium shifts forward (towards the side with more moles of aqueous particles, i.e., the right side) to counteract this drop, thus ionizing more acetic acid.
Λm = 48.15 S cm2 mol-1 | α = 0.1233 | Ka = 1.78 × 10-5

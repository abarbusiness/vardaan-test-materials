SECTION A (16 Marks)(Attempt all questions - 2 Marks Each)
1.
Define a 'Nation State' in the context of the early nationalist movements.
(2)
Solution:
A Nation State is formed when people living in the same territory develop a consciousness of belonging together by sharing a common language, culture, history, ethnicity, or religion.
2.
Name the book written by Harriet Beecher Stowe. What was its impact on the American people?
(2)
Solution:
Book: Uncle Tom's Cabin.
Impact: It strongly influenced the people of the Northern states to vehemently oppose slavery based on liberal ideals, widening the divide between the North and South.
3.
What is the maximum permissible strength of the Lok Sabha? How are these representatives distributed between states and union territories?
(2)
Solution:
Maximum Strength: 550 members.
Distribution: 530 representatives are elected from states, and 20 representatives are elected from union territories.
4.
Define the 'Concurrent List' and provide two examples of subjects included in it.
(2)
Solution:
Concurrent List: It contains matters over which both the central government and the state governments can make laws.
Examples: Education, Trade unions, Forests, Population control (Any two).
5.
Define the term 'intermolecular force of attraction' in the context of matter.
(2)
Solution:
Intermolecular force of attraction: It is the force of attraction existing between the constituent particles (atoms, molecules, or ions) of a substance that holds them together in solid, liquid, or gaseous states.
6.
State two key points of difference between melting and boiling.
(2)
Solution:
1. State change: Melting is the conversion from a solid to a liquid, whereas boiling is the conversion from a liquid to a gas.
2. Phenomenon: Melting occurs at a specific melting point; Boiling occurs at a specific boiling point and is a bulk phenomenon (involves the entire liquid).
7.
What immediate political event in 1860 led to the secession of 11 Southern states, sparking the American Civil War?
(2)
Solution:
The election of Abraham Lincoln, an anti-slavery Republican, as the President of the United States in 1860 immediately led to the secession of the 11 Southern states.
8.
Mention two important legislative powers/roles of the Governor in a state.
(2)
Solution:
1. All state bills passed by the legislature must go to the Governor for his/her assent to become an Act.
2. The Governor has the power to summon or dissolve the State Legislative Assembly.
SECTION B (24 Marks)(Short Answer Type - 3 Marks Each)
9.
Briefly explain the event known as the 'Boston Tea Party' (1773) and its significance.
(3)
Solution:
Boston Tea Party: In protest against the Tea Act (which gave the British East India Company a monopoly over tea), American colonists disguised themselves as Native Americans and dumped tea crates from a British ship into the sea.
Significance: This rebellious act marked the beginning of open hostility against British rule, eventually leading to the American Revolution.
10.
What were the core ideals established by the French Revolution? Briefly state the impact of this revolution on the rest of Europe.
(3)
Solution:
Core Ideals: Liberty, Equality, and Fraternity.
Impact on Europe: It permanently ended the absolute monarchy in France and spread modern democratic principles across the globe. It also sparked a wave of nationalism that directly led to the unification of nations like Italy and Germany.
11.
Outline the composition of the Rajya Sabha. Why is it considered a 'Permanent Body'?
(3)
Solution:
Composition: Maximum of 250 members. 238 members represent states and union territories (elected by MLAs), and 12 members are nominated by the President (distinguished in art, literature, science, etc.).
Permanent Body: It is called a permanent body because it cannot be dissolved. Members serve a 6-year term, and one-third of its members retire every two years, ensuring the House is never completely empty.
12.
Differentiate between an Ordinary Bill and a Money Bill on the basis of their introduction and the power of the Rajya Sabha.
(3)
Solution:
Ordinary Bill: Can be introduced in either the Lok Sabha or the Rajya Sabha. Both houses have equal power to debate, amend, and pass it.
Money Bill: Can be introduced only in the Lok Sabha. The Rajya Sabha has highly restricted powers; it can only discuss it and has a maximum of 14 days to accept it or offer recommendations (which the Lok Sabha can reject).
13.
Explain the process of sublimation on the basis of the kinetic theory of matter. Give one example of a sublimable substance.
(3)
Solution:
Sublimation: It is the direct conversion of a solid into a gas on heating, without passing through the liquid state.
Kinetic Theory: On heating, particles in the solid gain extreme kinetic energy and vibrate vigorously, completely overcoming the intermolecular forces of attraction to break free directly into the gaseous state.
Example: Naphthalene balls, Camphor, or Dry ice (Any one).
14.
Solids have a definite shape and volume, whereas gases have neither. Explain this observation using the kinetic theory of matter.
(3)
Solution:
Solids: They have the strongest intermolecular forces of attraction and negligible intermolecular spaces. Particles are closely packed and can only vibrate at their mean positions, giving them a definite shape and volume.
Gases: They have negligible intermolecular forces and very large intermolecular spaces. Particles have high kinetic energy and move freely in all directions, acquiring the shape and volume of whatever container they are in.
15.
Mention three key economic and social differences between the Northern and Southern states of America that eventually led to the Civil War.
(3)
Solution:
  1. Economy: The North was heavily industrialised with factories, whereas the South was largely rural and agricultural.
  2. Agriculture: The South depended almost entirely on large, labour-intensive cotton and sugar plantations.
  3. Slavery: The South relied heavily on the forced labour of African slaves for its plantations, whereas the North strongly opposed slavery based on liberal ideals.
16.
Outline the general 'Process Flow' of how a draft bill becomes an Act (law) in the Parliament.
(3)
Solution:
The process flow is as follows:
1. Draft Bill is introduced in the House (Lok Sabha or Rajya Sabha).
2. The bill is debated, discussed, and passed by both Houses.
3. The passed bill is sent to the President for assent (approval).
4. Upon receiving the President's signature, the bill becomes an Act.
SECTION C (20 Marks)(Long Answer Type - 5 Marks Each)
17.
Discuss in detail any three major causes that led to the outbreak of the French Revolution in 1789.
(5)
Solution:
1. Social Inequality: French society was rigidly divided into three Estates. The First Estate (Clergy) and Second Estate (Nobility) enjoyed immense wealth and privileges while paying no taxes. The Third Estate (95% of the population, comprising commoners and peasants) bore the entire burden of heavy taxes and had no political rights.
2. Economic Hardship: The royal treasury was emptied due to the luxurious, wasteful spending of King Louis XVI and Queen Marie Antoinette, alongside the heavy cost of foreign wars. This, combined with repeated famines, drove up the price of bread, leading to mass starvation and anger.
3. Influence of Philosophers & America: Thinkers like Voltaire and Rousseau spread radical new ideas of liberty, equality, and fraternity. Additionally, French soldiers returning from the American War of Independence brought back strong ideas of democracy, inspiring the French to revolt against absolute monarchy.
18.
(a) Who is the presiding officer of the Lok Sabha? (1 Mark)
(b) State the qualifications required for a candidate to become a Member of Parliament (MP) in the Lok Sabha. (4 Marks)
(5)
Solution:
(a) Presiding Officer: The Speaker is the presiding officer of the Lok Sabha.

(b) Qualifications for Lok Sabha MP:
1. Must be a citizen of India and a registered voter.
2. Must be at least 25 years of age.
3. Must not hold any office of profit under the government.
4. Must be mentally sound, not be an insolvent or bankrupt, and must not be a proclaimed criminal.
19.
(a) Draw a well-labelled flowchart showing the interconversion of the three states of matter. Mention the names of the processes involved. (3 Marks)
(b) How does an increase in temperature affect the intermolecular space and the kinetic energy of particles in a solid? (2 Marks)
(5)
Solution:
(a) Flowchart Details:
(Students must draw a diagram containing 'Solid', 'Liquid', 'Gas' with arrows pointing between them, labelled as follows:)
• Solid to Liquid: Melting/Fusion
• Liquid to Solid: Freezing/Solidification
• Liquid to Gas: Boiling/Vaporization

(b) Effect of Temperature Increase on a Solid:
1. Kinetic Energy: Increases, as the supplied thermal energy converts into kinetic energy, causing the particles to vibrate faster at their mean positions.
2. Intermolecular Space: Increases slightly (thermal expansion), as the vigorous vibrations push neighboring particles further apart.
20.
Explain the sequential changes in the state of matter when ice at -10°C is heated continuously until it turns into steam at 110°C. In your explanation, specifically refer to the kinetic energy of the particles and the intermolecular forces at 0°C and 100°C.
(5)
Solution:
Sequential Changes:
1. Ice at -10°C to 0°C: As heat is absorbed, the kinetic energy of ice molecules increases, and the temperature rises to 0°C. The state remains solid.
2. Melting at 0°C: The temperature remains constant. The heat supplied (latent heat of fusion) is used to overcome the strong intermolecular forces of attraction holding the ice lattice together. Kinetic energy is constant, but potential energy increases. Solid ice transitions into liquid water.
3. Water at 0°C to 100°C: The kinetic energy of liquid water molecules increases continuously, causing the temperature to rise up to 100°C. The state remains liquid.
4. Boiling at 100°C (and heating to 110°C): The temperature remains constant at 100°C during boiling. Heat (latent heat of vaporization) is utilized to completely break all remaining intermolecular forces. Liquid water transitions into gaseous steam. Further heating increases the kinetic energy of the steam particles, raising the temperature to 110°C.

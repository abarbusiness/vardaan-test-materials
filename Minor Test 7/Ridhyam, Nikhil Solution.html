SECTION B (15 Marks)
4.
Ans: Key Differences:
(a) Diffusion: Does not require any membrane to occur.
Osmosis: Strictly requires a semi-permeable membrane. [2 Marks]

(b) Cerebrum: Serves as the seat of intelligence, memory, reasoning, and controls voluntary activities.
Cerebellum: Coordinates muscular activity, posture, and body balance. [2 Marks]

(c) Xylem: Transports water and minerals unidirectionally (upward from roots).
Phloem: Transports food bidirectionally (downward from leaves and upward to buds/fruits). [2 Marks]
5.
Ans: Transpiration Pull:
As water evaporates continuously from the mesophyll cells of leaves via transpiration, a water deficit is created. [1.5 Marks]
This generates a massive suction force, known as the transpiration pull. It acts like a biological suction pump, pulling a continuous column of water up through the xylem vessels to the top of tall trees. [1.5 Marks]
6.
Ans: Functional categories of neurons:
1. Sensory Neurons: Transmit nerve impulses from the sensory receptors (sense organs) to the central nervous system. [1 Mark]
2. Motor Neurons: Carry response signals from the central nervous system to effector organs (muscles or glands). [1 Mark]
3. Association Neurons (Interneurons): Present in the brain/spinal cord; they process sensory input and relay it to motor neurons. [1 Mark]
7.
Ans: Reflex Arc Pathway:
The pathway of a reflex arc when touching a hot object is:

Stimulus (Heat)
    ↓
Receptor (Heat/Pain receptors in skin of hand) [0.5 Mark]
    ↓
Sensory Neuron [0.5 Mark]
    ↓
Spinal Cord (Association Neuron) [1 Mark]
    ↓
Motor Neuron [0.5 Mark]
    ↓
Effector (Arm muscles contract) → Response (Hand pulled away) [0.5 Mark]
8.
Ans: Structural adaptations of root hairs:
1. Enormous surface area: The numerous, fine outgrowths enormously increase the absorption surface area. [1 Mark]
2. Concentrated cell sap: The vacuole contains a highly concentrated solution of salts, maintaining a lower water concentration than soil to drive osmosis. [1 Mark]
3. Thin permeable walls: The cell wall is freely permeable and the cell membrane is selectively permeable, allowing easy inflow of water. [1 Mark]

(b) Physiological function:
(i) Dendrites: Receive nerve impulses from sensory receptors or neighboring neurons and transmit them to the cyton. [1 Mark]
(ii) Axon: Transmits nerve impulses away from the cell body to another neuron or effector organ. [1 Mark]

Ans: Human Brain & Spinal Cord:
(a) Three primary subdivisions:
1. Forebrain (Cerebrum & Diencephalon)
2. Midbrain
3. Hindbrain (Cerebellum, Pons, Medulla oblongata)
[1.5 Marks: 0.5 Mark for each]

(b) Respective functions:
(i) Cerebrum: Controls thinking, memory, intelligence, and voluntary actions. [1 Mark]
(ii) Cerebellum: Coordinates voluntary muscle movements and maintains body balance. [1 Mark]
(iii) Medulla Oblongata: Controls involuntary, life-sustaining functions like heartbeat, breathing, and swallowing. [1 Mark]

(c) Spinal Cord Location: It lies protected inside the neural canal of the vertebral column (backbone). [0.5 Mark]
11.
Ans: Transpiration factors & Wilting:
(a) Transpiration: The loss of water in the form of water vapor from the aerial parts of a plant. [1.5 Marks]
External Factors (Any three):
1. Temperature: Higher temperature speeds up evaporation, increasing transpiration.
2. Sunlight: Bright light causes stomata to open, increasing transpiration.
3. Humidity: High moisture in the air decreases the rate of transpiration.
4. Wind: Moving air removes water vapor around leaves, increasing transpiration.
[1.5 Marks: 0.5 Mark for each of the three named factors with a brief effect]

(b) Wilting: On hot summer afternoons, the rate of transpiration far exceeds the rate at which roots can absorb water from the soil. [1 Mark]
This causes cells to lose turgor pressure and become flaccid, making the soft stems and leaves droop down temporarily. [1 Mark]

Voluntary ActionsInvoluntary Actions

(b) Stimulus: Any detectable change in the environment that evokes a physiological response in an organism. [0.5 Mark]
Receptor: A specialized cell or tissue sensitive to a particular type of stimulus. [0.5 Mark]
(i) Hearing: Phonoreceptors (in the ear). [0.5 Mark]
(ii) Vision: Photoreceptors (in the eye). [0.5 Mark]

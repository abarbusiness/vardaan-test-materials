SECTION B (15 Marks)
4.
Ans: Key Differences:
(a) Xylem: Transports water and dissolved minerals upwards from the roots.
Phloem: Transports manufactured food (sugars) bidirectionally from leaves to other parts. [1.5 Marks]

(b) Bone: Matrix is hard, rigid, and rich in calcium/phosphorus salts (non-flexible).
Cartilage: Matrix is rubbery, semi-solid, and rich in proteins (flexible). [1.5 Marks]
5.
Ans: Characteristics of Meristematic tissues:
1. Cells are small, closely packed with no intercellular spaces. [1 Mark]
2. They have thin cell walls, dense cytoplasm, and a prominent nucleus. [1 Mark]
3. The cells continuously divide to produce new cells, and usually lack vacuoles. [1 Mark]
6.
Ans: Types of muscular tissue:
Three types are: 1. Striated (Skeletal) muscle, 2. Unstriated (Smooth) muscle, 3. Cardiac muscle. [1.5 Marks: 0.5 each]
Unique feature of cardiac muscle: They are structurally striated and branched but functionally involuntary, and they contract continuously without ever getting fatigued. [1.5 Marks]
7.
Ans: Blood components:
Fluid matrix: Plasma. [0.5 Mark]
Three cellular components: Red Blood Cells (RBCs), White Blood Cells (WBCs), and Platelets. [1.5 Marks: 0.5 each]
RBC function: Transport oxygen from the lungs to body tissues with the help of haemoglobin. [1 Mark]
8.
Ans: Collenchyma vs. Sclerenchyma:
Collenchyma: Consists of living cells with cell walls thickened unevenly at the corners due to pectin. [1.5 Marks]
Sclerenchyma: Consists of dead cells with heavily thickened walls completely coated with lignin. [1.5 Marks]
SECTION C (20 Marks)
9.
Ans: Neuron Structure & Function:
(a) Neuron Diagram:
Drawing must clearly depict the cell body (Cyton), branching Dendrites, central Nucleus, a long Axon, and Axon terminals.
[3 Marks: 1.5 for a neat, accurate structure + 1.5 for correctly labelling at least 3 parts]

(b) Function of nervous tissue: It is highly specialized to receive stimuli and rapidly transmit electrical messages (nerve impulses) from one part of the body to another, enabling the animal to respond to its environment. [2 Marks]
10.
Ans: Complex Permanent Tissue:
(a) Definition: Tissues that are made up of more than one type of cells that coordinate together to perform a common function are called complex permanent tissues. [1 Mark]
Two types: Xylem and Phloem. [1 Mark]

(b) Four elements of Phloem: Sieve tubes, Companion cells, Phloem parenchyma, and Phloem fibres. [2 Marks: 0.5 each]
Dead element: Phloem fibres (Phloem sclerenchyma). [1 Mark]
11.
Ans: Stomata & Muscle Differences:
(a) Stomata: They are tiny pore-like structures present in the epidermis of leaves. [1 Mark]
Function: They facilitate the exchange of gases (CO₂ and O₂) and allow water loss via transpiration. [1 Mark]

(b) Voluntary vs. Involuntary Muscles:
Voluntary (Striated) MusclesInvoluntary (Smooth) Muscles
Under the conscious control of our will.Not under our conscious control.
Cells are cylindrical and unbranched.Cells are spindle-shaped.
They possess distinct light and dark bands (striations).They do not have any striations.
[3 Marks: 1 Mark for each contrasting point]
12.
Ans: Connective Tissue & Blood:
(a) Function: Connective tissue binds, supports, and packages various organs and tissues of the body together. [1 Mark]
Examples of supportive connective tissue: Bone and Cartilage. [2 Marks: 1 Mark each]

(b) Blood as Fluid Connective Tissue: It is called a connective tissue because it originates from mesoderm and connects different parts of the body by transporting substances between them. [1 Mark] It is called 'fluid' because its matrix (plasma) is completely liquid, allowing its cells to flow freely rather than being rigidly fixed. [1 Mark]

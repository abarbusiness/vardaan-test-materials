SECTION B (15 Marks)
4.
Ans:
Differences:
(a) Apical Meristem: Located at the growing tips of stems, branches, and roots.
Lateral Meristem: Located along the lateral sides (cambium) of stems and roots. [1 Mark]

(b) Parenchyma: Consists of thin-walled, living cells.
Sclerenchyma: Consists of thick-walled, dead cells at maturity. [1 Mark]

(c) Tendon: Connects skeletal muscles to bones, facilitating movement.
Ligament: Connects bones to other bones at joints, providing stability. [1 Mark]
5.
Ans:
The three main types of muscle tissues are:
1. Striated / Skeletal Muscles: Long, cylindrical, unbranched, and multinucleated cells with light and dark bands. They are voluntary in action. [1 Mark]
2. Non-Striated / Smooth Muscles: Spindle-shaped, uninucleated cells without any striations. They are involuntary in action. [1 Mark]
3. Cardiac Muscles: Cylindrical, branched, uninucleated cells with weak striations. They are involuntary, rhythmic, and never get fatigued (found only in heart walls). [1 Mark]
6.
Ans:
Protective Tissue: These tissues form a protective covering over various plant organs to shield them against mechanical injury, extreme temperatures, and invasion by pathogens. [1 Mark]

- Epidermis: The outermost layer of cells covering young leaves, stems, and roots. It is covered by a waxy, waterproof cuticle which minimizes water loss through evaporation. [1 Mark]
- Cork: Formed in older stems and roots, replacing the epidermis. Cork cells are dead, compactly arranged without intercellular spaces, and contain a chemical called suberin which makes them impermeable to water and gases. [1 Mark]
7.
Ans:
Three important functions of epithelial tissue are:
1. Protection: It covers body surfaces and protects internal tissues against mechanical injury, chemical exposure, and bacterial invasion. [1 Mark]
2. Absorption: It lines the digestive tract (such as stomach and small intestine) to absorb nutrients and water. [1 Mark]
3. Secretion: Specialized epithelial cells in glands secrete substances like sweat, saliva, mucus, enzymes, and hormones. [1 Mark]
8.
Ans:
Composition of Mammalian Blood:
- Liquid Matrix: Plasma (straw-colored, non-living liquid part constituting about 55% of blood). [1 Mark]
- Three types of blood cells (Formed elements): [2 Marks]
  1. Red Blood Cells (RBCs / Erythrocytes): Disc-shaped, lack a nucleus (at maturity), and carry oxygen via hemoglobin. [½ Mark]
  2. White Blood Cells (WBCs / Leukocytes): Irregularly shaped, nucleated, and help the body fight infections. [½ Mark]
  3. Platelets (Thrombocytes): Tiny cell fragments that play a critical role in blood clotting. [1 Mark -> ½ Mark for mentioning + ½ Mark for role]
SECTION C (20 Marks)
9.
Ans:
(a) Complex Permanent Tissues: Tissues made up of more than one type of cell that coordinate together to perform a common specialized function. [1 Mark]
The two types are Xylem and Phloem. [1 Mark]

(b) Four elements of Xylem: Vessels, Tracheids, Xylem Parenchyma, and Xylem Fibres. [1 Mark]
Water Conduction: Xylem vessels and tracheids are dead, hollow tubes with thick lignified walls. They form a continuous pipeline from roots to leaves. Water absorbed by root hairs enters xylem and is pulled upwards unidirectionally, driven majorly by the suction pull created by transpiration (loss of water vapor from leaves). [2 Marks]
10.
Ans:
(a) Diagram of a Neuron: Student's drawing must show a star-shaped cell body (cyton) with branching dendrites, a single long axon covered with myelin sheath, and terminal nerve endings.
[3 Marks: 1.5 Marks for neat structure, 1.5 Marks for correctly labeling Cyton, Dendrites, Axon, and Myelin sheath]

(b) Functions of Neuron Parts:
(i) Dendrites: Receive nerve impulses or stimuli from sensory receptors or neighboring neurons and transmit them to the cyton. [1 Mark]
(ii) Axon: Carries the nerve impulse away from the cyton to the nerve endings/synapse to be passed to the next cell. [1 Mark]
11.
Ans:
(a) Identified Plant Tissue: Collenchyma. [1 Mark]
Main Function: Provides mechanical strength, elasticity, and flexibility to young plants and organs (like leaf stalks), allowing them to bend easily in strong winds without breaking. [1 Mark]

(b) Cartilage vs. Bone:
- Matrix Composition: Bone has a hard, rigid, porous matrix rich in inorganic calcium and phosphorus salts. Cartilage has a firm, non-porous, elastic matrix composed of proteins and sugars. [1.5 Marks]
- Flexibility: Bone is tough and completely rigid/non-flexible, whereas cartilage is semi-rigid and highly flexible/pliable.

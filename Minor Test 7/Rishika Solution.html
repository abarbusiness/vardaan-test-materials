SECTION B (15 Marks)
4.
Ans: Differences in Nutrition:
Autotrophic Nutrition: The mode of nutrition in which organisms make their own food from simple substances like carbon dioxide and water. Example: Green plants. [1.5 Marks]
Heterotrophic Nutrition: The mode of nutrition in which organisms cannot make their own food and depend on plants or other animals for food. Example: Humans, animals. [1.5 Marks]
5.
Ans: Villi:
Villi are thousands of finger-like outgrowths. [1 Mark]
Location: They are located on the inner walls of the small intestine. [1 Mark]
Function: They significantly increase the surface area for the rapid absorption of digested food materials into the blood vessels. [1 Mark]
6.
Ans: Feeding and digestion in Amoeba:
When Amoeba senses food, it pushes out pseudopodia (false feet) around the food particle and engulfs it. [1 Mark]
The food becomes trapped in a food vacuole. [1 Mark]
Digestive juices are secreted into the food vacuole. They act on the food and break it down into simpler substances which are then absorbed. [1 Mark]
7.
Ans: Leguminous crops and farmers:
Farmers grow leguminous crops because their roots contain nodules that house a symbiotic bacterium called Rhizobium. [1 Mark]
Rhizobium can take atmospheric nitrogen and convert it into a usable, soluble form for the plants. [1 Mark]
This naturally replenishes the soil with nitrogen, reducing the need for farmers to add artificial nitrogenous fertilizers. [1 Mark]
8.
Ans: Rumination:
Rumination: The process by which grass-eating animals quickly swallow grass, store it in the rumen where it is partially digested (cud), and later return this cud to the mouth in small lumps to chew it thoroughly. [2 Marks]
Examples: Cow, Buffalo, Goat, Sheep (Any two). [1 Mark: 0.5 each]

9.
Ans: Digestive System & Glands:
(a) Diagram: The student must draw a neat diagram of the human digestive tract.
[3 Marks: 1.5 for neat/accurate drawing + 1.5 for correctly labelling at least 4 organs (e.g., Stomach, Liver, Pancreas, Small Intestine, Large Intestine)]

(b) Liver: Secretes bile juice (stored in the gall bladder) which plays an important role in the digestion/emulsification of fats. [1 Mark]
Pancreas: Secretes pancreatic juice which acts on carbohydrates, fats, and proteins and changes them into simpler forms. [1 Mark]
10.
Ans: Heterotrophic Nutrition in Plants:
(a) Four types of heterotrophic nutrition in plants:
1. Parasitic: They derive nutrition from a living host plant (e.g., Cuscuta).
2. Saprotrophic: They secrete digestive juices on dead/decaying matter and absorb the solution (e.g., Mushroom/Fungi).
3. Insectivorous: They trap and digest insects to fulfill their nutritional needs (e.g., Pitcher plant).
4. Symbiotic: Two organisms live together and share shelter and nutrients (e.g., Lichens).
[4 Marks: 1 Mark for each type with correct example]

(b) Why insectivorous plants trap insects: They grow in soil that is deficient in nitrogen. They digest insects specifically to fulfill their nitrogen requirements. [1 Mark]
11.
Ans: Five Major Steps of Animal Nutrition:
1. Ingestion: The process of taking food into the body through the mouth. [1 Mark]
2. Digestion: The breakdown of complex food substances into simpler, soluble forms. [1 Mark]
3. Absorption: The process by which digested food passes into the blood vessels in the wall of the intestine. [1 Mark]
4. Assimilation: The transport of absorbed substances via blood to different organs of the body where they are used to build complex substances (for energy, growth, and repair). [1 Mark]
5. Egestion: The process of passing out the undigested and unabsorbed food residues (faeces) from the body. [1 Mark]
12.
Ans: Mouth & Pitcher Plant:
(a) Role of Mouth: The buccal cavity is where digestion begins. Teeth chew and break down the food mechanically. Salivary glands secrete saliva which mixes with food and breaks down starch into sugars. The tongue helps in mixing and swallowing. [2 Marks]
Types of teeth: Incisors, Canines, Premolars, and Molars. [1 Mark for all four]

(b) Pitcher plant mechanism: The leaf is modified into a pitcher-like structure with a lid that can open and close. [1 Mark]
Inside the pitcher, there are downward-pointing hairs. When an insect lands, the lid closes, and the trapped insect gets entangled in the hairs and is digested by digestive juices secreted in the pitcher. [1 Mark]

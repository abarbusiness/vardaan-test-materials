Surprise Test - Class 12 Physics (Chapter 2: Electrostatic Potential and Capacitance)

SECTION A
1. Two equipotential surfaces can never intersect each other. Justify this statement with a valid scientific reason.
[2 Marks]

Solution:

If two equipotential surfaces were to intersect, it would mean that at the point of intersection, there would be two different values of electric potential, which is physically impossible. (1 Mark)

Alternatively: The direction of the electric field is always perpendicular to an equipotential surface. If they intersect, there would be two perpendiculars at the point of intersection, implying two different directions of the electric field at a single point, which is impossible. (1 Mark)

2. Write the expression for the potential energy of an electric dipole of dipole moment \( \vec{p} \) placed in a uniform electric field \( \vec{E} \). Identify the conditions for stable and unstable equilibrium.
[2 Marks]

Solution:

The potential energy \( U \) of an electric dipole is given by the dot product of dipole moment and electric field:
\[ U = -\vec{p} \cdot \vec{E} = -pE \cos\theta \] (1 Mark)

Stable Equilibrium: Occurs when potential energy is minimum. This happens when the dipole is aligned parallel to the electric field (\( \theta = 0^\circ \)). Here, \( U = -pE \). (1/2 Mark)

Unstable Equilibrium: Occurs when potential energy is maximum. This happens when the dipole is aligned anti-parallel to the field (\( \theta = 180^\circ \)). Here, \( U = +pE \). (1/2 Mark)

3. A charge of \( 5\mu C \) is moved from a point \( A \) to a point \( B \) on an equipotential surface. What is the work done by the electrostatic force in this process? Explain briefly.
[2 Marks]

Solution:

The work done is Zero. (1 Mark)

Reason: By definition, the electric potential is constant everywhere on an equipotential surface. Therefore, the potential difference between any two points A and B on it is zero (\( V_B - V_A = 0 \)).
Since Work Done \( W = q(V_B - V_A) \), we get \( W = 5\mu C \times 0 = 0 \). (1 Mark)

4. Draw the equipotential surfaces for a single positive point charge. How does the spacing between the surfaces change as we move away from the charge?
[2 Marks]

Solution:

(Diagram Requirement: Student must draw concentric circles/spheres around a central '+' charge, with outward radial electric field lines perpendicular to these surfaces.) (1 Mark)

Spacing: The electric field intensity decreases as we move away from the point charge (\( E \propto \frac{1}{r^2} \)). We know the relation \( E = -\frac{dV}{dr} \), which gives \( dr = -\frac{dV}{E} \).
For a constant potential difference \( dV \), the spacing \( dr \) is inversely proportional to \( E \). Since \( E \) decreases, the spacing between equipotential surfaces increases as we move away from the charge. (1 Mark)

5. Establish the relationship between electric field intensity (\( E \)) and electric potential (\( V \)) at a point.
[2 Marks]

Solution:

Consider a unit positive test charge moved by a small distance \( d\vec{r} \) against an electric field \( \vec{E} \).

The work done against the electric field is:
\( dW = \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r} = (1)\vec{E} \cdot d\vec{r} = -E dr \) (Since force is against the field, angle is 180°, so \( \cos 180^\circ = -1 \)) (1 Mark)

By definition, the work done in moving a unit positive charge is the potential difference \( dV \).
Equating the two: \( dV = -E dr \)
\[ E = -\frac{dV}{dr} \] (1 Mark)

(The negative sign indicates that the direction of the electric field is in the direction of steepest decrease of electrical potential.)

SECTION B
6. Derive an expression for the electrostatic potential energy of a system of two point charges \( q_1 \) and \( q_2 \) separated by a distance \( r \) in the absence of any external electric field.
[3 Marks]

Solution:

Let the first charge \( q_1 \) be brought from infinity to a point \( P_1 \). Since there is initially no external electric field, the work done is zero:
\( W_1 = 0 \). (1 Mark)

This charge \( q_1 \) creates an electric potential at a distance \( r \) (where point \( P_2 \) is located):

\[ V_1 = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q_1}{r} \]

Now, to bring the second charge \( q_2 \) from infinity to point \( P_2 \), work must be done against the electric field of \( q_1 \). (1 Mark)

\( W_2 = q_2 \times V_1 \)
\[ W_2 = q_2 \left( \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q_1}{r} \right) \]
\[ W_2 = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q_1 q_2}{r} \]

The total work done (\( W_1 + W_2 \)) is stored as the electrostatic potential energy (\( U \)) of the system.
\[ U = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q_1 q_2}{r} \] (1 Mark)

7. Two capacitors of capacitances \( 3 \mu F \) and \( 6 \mu F \) are connected in series across a \( 12V \) battery. Calculate:
(i) The equivalent capacitance of the combination.
(ii) The potential difference across the \( 3 \mu F \) capacitor.
[3 Marks]

Solution:

(i) Equivalent Capacitance (\( C_{eq} \)):

For capacitors connected in series, the formula is: \( \frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} \)
\( \frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{6} = \frac{2+1}{6} = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2} \mu F^{-1} \)
\( C_{eq} = 2 \mu F \) (1.5 Marks)

(ii) Potential Difference across \( 3 \mu F \) capacitor (\( V_1 \)):

In a series combination, the charge \( Q \) is the same on both capacitors.
Total charge drawn from the battery: \( Q = C_{eq} \times V = 2 \mu F \times 12 V = 24 \mu C \) (0.5 Marks)

Potential difference across the first capacitor (\( C_1 = 3 \mu F \)):
\( V_1 = \frac{Q}{C_1} = \frac{24 \mu C}{3 \mu F} \)
\( V_1 = 8 V \) (1 Mark)

8. Derive the expression for the energy stored in a parallel plate capacitor of capacitance \( C \) charged to a potential \( V \).
[3 Marks]

Solution:

Consider a capacitor of capacitance \( C \). Initially, it is uncharged. Let it be charged gradually by a battery.

At any intermediate stage during charging, let the instantaneous charge on the capacitor be \( q \) and its potential difference be \( v \).
Then, \( v = \frac{q}{C} \). (1 Mark)

The small amount of work done (\( dW \)) in transferring an additional small charge \( dq \) against this potential difference is:
\[ dW = v \cdot dq = \frac{q}{C} dq \] (1 Mark)

The total work done to charge the capacitor from an initial charge \( q = 0 \) to a final charge \( q = Q \) is the integral of \( dW \):
\[ W = \int_0^Q \frac{q}{C} dq = \frac{1}{C} \left[ \frac{q^2}{2} \right]_0^Q = \frac{Q^2}{2C} \]

This work done is stored inside the capacitor as electrostatic potential energy \( U \).
\( U = \frac{Q^2}{2C} \).
Substituting \( Q = CV \), we get the alternate form: \( U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 \). (1 Mark)

9. A parallel plate capacitor is charged to a certain potential difference by a battery. The battery is then disconnected, and a dielectric slab of dielectric constant \( K \) is inserted, completely filling the space between the plates. State the effect (with reasoning) on:
(i) The capacitance of the capacitor.
(ii) The electric field between the plates.
(iii) The energy stored in the capacitor.
[3 Marks]

Solution:

Crucial Concept: Since the battery is disconnected before inserting the dielectric, the Total Charge (\( Q \)) remains constant.

(i) Capacitance:
The presence of a dielectric medium always increases the capacitance by a factor of its dielectric constant \( K \).
\( C' = K \cdot C_0 \) (Capacitance increases \( K \) times). (1 Mark)

(ii) Electric Field:
The new potential difference is \( V' = \frac{Q}{C'} = \frac{Q}{KC_0} = \frac{V_0}{K} \).
Since Electric Field \( E = \frac{V}{d} \), the new electric field is \( E' = \frac{V'}{d} = \frac{V_0/K}{d} = \frac{E_0}{K} \).
\( E' = \frac{E_0}{K} \) (Electric field decreases by a factor of \( K \)). (1 Mark)

(iii) Energy stored:
Using the energy formula that involves constant charge \( Q \):
\( U' = \frac{Q^2}{2C'} = \frac{Q^2}{2(KC_0)} = \frac{1}{K} \left( \frac{Q^2}{2C_0} \right) = \frac{U_0}{K} \)
\( U' = \frac{U_0}{K} \) (Stored energy decreases by a factor of \( K \)). (1 Mark)

10. Three point charges \( +q \), \( -2q \), and \( +3q \) are placed at the vertices \( A \), \( B \), and \( C \) respectively of an equilateral triangle of side \( a \). Find the total electrostatic potential energy of the system.
[3 Marks]

Solution:

The total electrostatic potential energy of a system of charges is the algebraic sum of the potential energies of all possible pairs of charges.

\[ U_{total} = U_{AB} + U_{BC} + U_{CA} \] (1 Mark)

\[ U_{total} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \left[ \frac{(q_{A})(q_{B})}{r_{AB}} + \frac{(q_{B})(q_{C})}{r_{BC}} + \frac{(q_{C})(q_{A})}{r_{CA}} \right] \]

Here, \( r_{AB} = r_{BC} = r_{CA} = a \). Substituting the charges:
\[ U_{total} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \left[ \frac{(+q)(-2q)}{a} + \frac{(-2q)(+3q)}{a} + \frac{(+3q)(+q)}{a} \right] \] (1 Mark)

\[ U_{total} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0 a} \left[ -2q^2 - 6q^2 + 3q^2 \right] \]

\[ U_{total} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0 a} \left[ -5q^2 \right] \]

\[ U_{total} = -\frac{5q^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 a} \] (1 Mark)
SECTION C
11. (a) Derive an expression for the capacitance of a parallel plate capacitor of plate area \( A \) and plate separation \( d \) when the space between the plates is completely filled with a vacuum. (2 Marks)

(b) A dielectric slab of thickness \( t \) (\( t < d \)) and dielectric constant \( K \) is introduced between the plates of the above capacitor. Obtain the new expression for its capacitance. (3 Marks)
[5 Marks]

Solution:

(a) Capacitance in Vacuum [2 Marks]:

Let the plates have surface charge densities \( +\sigma \) and \( -\sigma \), where \( \sigma = \frac{Q}{A} \).

The uniform electric field between the plates in vacuum is:
\( E_0 = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0} = \frac{Q}{A\epsilon_0} \) (0.5 Mark)

The potential difference between the plates is:
\( V_0 = E_0 \cdot d = \frac{Q \cdot d}{A\epsilon_0} \) (0.5 Mark)

By definition, capacitance is the ratio of charge to potential:
\( C_0 = \frac{Q}{V_0} = \frac{Q}{\left(\frac{Q \cdot d}{A\epsilon_0}\right)} \)

\[ C_0 = \frac{\epsilon_0 A}{d} \] (1 Mark)

(b) Capacitance with Dielectric Slab (\( t < d \)) [3 Marks]:

When a dielectric slab of thickness \( t \) is inserted, the space between the plates consists of two regions:
1. Vacuum region of thickness \( (d-t) \) with electric field \( E_0 = \frac{Q}{A\epsilon_0} \).
2. Dielectric region of thickness \( t \) with reduced electric field \( E = \frac{E_0}{K} \). (1 Mark)

The total potential difference \( V \) across the plates is the sum of the potential drops across these two regions:
\[ V = E_0(d-t) + E(t) \]
\[ V = E_0(d-t) + \frac{E_0}{K}(t) \]
\[ V = E_0 \left[ (d-t) + \frac{t}{K} \right] \] (1 Mark)

Substitute \( E_0 = \frac{Q}{A\epsilon_0} \):
\[ V = \frac{Q}{A\epsilon_0} \left[ d - t + \frac{t}{K} \right] \]

The new Capacitance is \( C = \frac{Q}{V} \):
\[ C = \frac{Q}{\frac{Q}{A\epsilon_0} \left( d - t + \frac{t}{K} \right)} \]

\[ C = \frac{\epsilon_0 A}{d - t + \frac{t}{K}} \] (1 Mark)

12. (a) Define an electric dipole. Find the expression for the electric potential at a point on the axial line of an electric dipole. (3 Marks)

(b) Show that the electric potential at any point on the equatorial line of an electric dipole is zero. (2 Marks)
[5 Marks]

Solution:

(a) Definition & Axial Potential [3 Marks]:

Electric Dipole: An electric dipole is a pair of equal and opposite point charges separated by a small vector distance. (0.5 Mark)

Let an electric dipole consist of charges \( -q \) at point A and \( +q \) at point B separated by a distance \( 2a \). Let P be a point on the axial line at a distance \( r \) from the center O of the dipole on the side of \( +q \).

Potential at P due to charge \( +q \): \( V_1 = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{(r-a)} \)
Potential at P due to charge \( -q \): \( V_2 = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{-q}{(r+a)} \) (1 Mark)

By superposition, total Potential \( V \) at P is the algebraic sum:
\[ V = V_1 + V_2 = \frac{q}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \left[ \frac{1}{r-a} - \frac{1}{r+a} \right] \]
\[ V = \frac{q}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \left[ \frac{(r+a) - (r-a)}{(r-a)(r+a)} \right] \]
\[ V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q(2a)}{r^2 - a^2} \]

Since the electric dipole moment is \( p = q \times 2a \), we get:
\[ V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{p}{r^2 - a^2} \] (1.5 Marks)

(For a short dipole where \( r \gg a \), this reduces to \( V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{p}{r^2} \))

(b) Equatorial Potential [2 Marks]:

Let point P be on the equatorial line (perpendicular bisector) of the dipole at a distance \( r \) from the center O.

The distance of point P from both the charges \( -q \) (at A) and \( +q \) (at B) is exactly the same, given by Pythagoras theorem: \( AP = BP = \sqrt{r^2 + a^2} \). (1 Mark)

Total potential at P is the sum of potentials due to both charges:
\[ V = V_{+q} + V_{-q} \]
\[ V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{\sqrt{r^2 + a^2}} + \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{-q}{\sqrt{r^2 + a^2}} \]
\[ V = 0 \] (1 Mark)

Therefore, the entire equatorial plane of an electric dipole is a zero-potential surface.

13. A network of four capacitors \( C_1 = 10 \mu F \), \( C_2 = 20 \mu F \), \( C_3 = 30 \mu F \), and \( C_4 = 40 \mu F \) are connected to a \( 100V \) DC supply. \( C_1 \), \( C_2 \), and \( C_3 \) are connected in parallel, and this combination is connected in series with \( C_4 \).

(a) Draw the circuit diagram for the given arrangement. (1 Mark)
(b) Calculate the equivalent capacitance of the network. (2 Marks)
(c) Calculate the total charge supplied by the battery. (1 Mark)
(d) Calculate the energy stored in the capacitor \( C_4 \). (1 Mark)
[5 Marks]

Solution:

(a) Circuit Diagram [1 Mark]:

(Diagram Requirement: Student must neatly sketch a circuit where three capacitors \( C_1 \), \( C_2 \), and \( C_3 \) are in parallel branches. These branches merge into a single node, which then connects to \( C_4 \). The entire combination is closed in a loop with a 100V DC source.)

(b) Equivalent Capacitance (\( C_{eq} \)) [2 Marks]:

First, resolve the parallel combination of \( C_1, C_2, C_3 \):
\( C_p = C_1 + C_2 + C_3 = 10\mu F + 20\mu F + 30\mu F = 60 \mu F \) (1 Mark)

Now, this equivalent capacitance \( C_p \) is in series with \( C_4 \):
\( \frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{1}{C_p} + \frac{1}{C_4} = \frac{1}{60} + \frac{1}{40} \)
\( \frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{2 + 3}{120} = \frac{5}{120} \)
\( C_{eq} = \frac{120}{5} = 24 \mu F \) (1 Mark)

(c) Total Charge (\( Q \)) [1 Mark]:

The total charge supplied by the 100V battery to the entire network is:
\( Q_{total} = C_{eq} \times V \)
\( Q_{total} = 24 \mu F \times 100 V \)
\( Q_{total} = 2400 \mu C \) (or \( 2.4 \times 10^{-3} C \)) (1 Mark)

(d) Energy stored in \( C_4 \) [1 Mark]:

Since \( C_4 \) is in series with the main circuit, the entire total charge \( Q_{total} \) flows through it.
Therefore, the charge on \( C_4 \) is \( Q_4 = 2400 \mu C \).

Energy stored in \( C_4 \) is:
\( U_4 = \frac{Q_4^2}{2C_4} \)
\( U_4 = \frac{(2400 \times 10^{-6} C)^2}{2 \times 40 \times 10^{-6} F} \)
\( U_4 = \frac{5760000 \times 10^{-12}}{80 \times 10^{-6}} \)
\( U_4 = 72000 \times 10^{-6} J = 0.072 J \) (1 Mark)

SECTION A: Electric Dipole & Equilibrium
1.
What is the SI unit of electric dipole moment?
(A) Coulomb per meter (C/m)
(B) Coulomb meter (C m)
(C) Coulomb meter squared (C m2)
(D) Newton meter (N m)
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
Electric dipole moment (p) is defined as the product of the magnitude of either charge (q) and the distance between them (2a). So, p = q × 2a. The SI unit of charge is Coulomb (C) and of distance is meter (m). Therefore, the SI unit is Coulomb meter (C m).
2.
By convention, what is the direction of the electric dipole moment vector p?
(A) From positive charge to negative charge
(B) From negative charge to positive charge
(C) Perpendicular to the line joining the charges
(D) It is a scalar quantity and has no direction
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
By convention in physics, the electric dipole moment vector is directed from the negative charge (-q) to the positive charge (+q) along the axis of the dipole. (Note: In chemistry, the convention is often reversed, but in physics, it is strictly -q to +q).
3.
What is the net electric charge on an electric dipole?
(A) +q
(B) -q
(C) Zero
(D) Infinity
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
An electric dipole consists of a pair of equal and opposite point charges, +q and -q, separated by a small distance. Therefore, the total or net charge of the system is (+q) + (-q) = 0.
4.
For a short electric dipole, the electric field E at an axial point at distance r from the center is proportional to:
(A) 1 / r
(B) 1 / r2
(C) 1 / r3
(D) r2
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
The formula for the electric field at an axial point of a short dipole (where r >> a) is given by E = 1/(4πε0) × (2p/r3). Thus, the electric field is inversely proportional to the cube of the distance, i.e., E ∝ 1/r3.
5.
Which of the following represents the correct vector formula for the torque τ experienced by a dipole in a uniform electric field E?
(A) τ = p • E
(B) τ = E × p
(C) τ = p × E
(D) τ = p / E
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
The torque on a dipole is given by the cross product of the dipole moment vector and the electric field vector. The magnitude is τ = pE sinθ. Using the right-hand rule for cross products, the direction of torque perfectly aligns with τ = p × E.
6.
An electric dipole is placed in a uniform electric field. The dipole is in a state of stable equilibrium when the angle between its dipole moment p and the electric field E is:
(A) 0°
(B) 90°
(C) 180°
(D) 45°
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (A)
For stable equilibrium, the potential energy must be minimum, and net force and torque must be zero. Potential energy U = -pE cosθ. It is minimum when cosθ = 1, which occurs at θ = 0°. At this position, the dipole is aligned parallel to the field.
7.
The torque acting on an electric dipole in a uniform electric field is maximum when the angle between p and E is:
(A) 0°
(B) 90°
(C) 180°
(D) 360°
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
The magnitude of torque is τ = pE sinθ. The value of sinθ is maximum at 90° (where sin(90°) = 1). Therefore, the torque is maximum when the dipole is placed perpendicular to the electric field.
8.
The ratio of the electric field intensity of a short dipole at a point on the axial line to the field at a point on the equatorial line, at the same distance from the center, is:
(A) 1 : 1
(B) 1 : 2
(C) 2 : 1
(D) 4 : 1
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
Eaxial = 1/(4πε0) × (2p/r3)
Eequatorial = 1/(4πε0) × (p/r3)
Taking the ratio, Eaxial / Eequatorial = 2/1. Therefore, the ratio is 2 : 1.
9.
A dipole of dipole moment p is originally placed parallel to a uniform electric field E. The work done in rotating it to an antiparallel position (180°) is:
(A) Zero
(B) pE
(C) 2pE
(D) -pE
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
Work done W = pE(cosθ1 - cosθ2).
Here, initially parallel means θ1 = 0° and finally antiparallel means θ2 = 180°.
W = pE(cos 0° - cos 180°) = pE(1 - (-1)) = pE(1 + 1) = 2pE.
10.
In a non-uniform electric field, an electric dipole generally experiences:
(A) Only a net force
(B) Only a net torque
(C) Both a net force and a net torque
(D) Neither a net force nor a net torque
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
In a non-uniform electric field, the field strength is different at the locations of the +q and -q charges. Therefore, the forces F1 = +qE1 and F2 = -qE2 are unequal in magnitude, resulting in a net translational force. Additionally, because the line of action of these forces does not coincide (unless aligned perfectly along the field lines), they will produce a net torque.
SECTION B: Continuous Charge & Gauss's Law
11.
What is the SI unit of surface charge density (σ)?
(A) Coulomb per meter (C/m)
(B) Coulomb per square meter (C/m2)
(C) Coulomb per cubic meter (C/m3)
(D) Coulomb meter (C m)
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
Surface charge density (σ) is defined as charge per unit area, i.e., σ = Q / A. The SI unit for charge is Coulomb (C) and for area is square meters (m2). Hence, the unit is C/m2.
12.
Electric flux is defined as:
(A) A vector quantity representing electric force.
(B) A scalar quantity representing the total number of electric field lines passing normally through an area.
(C) The product of charge and distance.
(D) The potential difference across a surface.
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
Electric flux (ΦE = E • A) is the dot product of two vectors (Electric Field and Area vector), making it a scalar quantity. Physically, it measures the total number of electric field lines passing perpendicular (normally) to a given surface area.
13.
Gauss's Law in electrostatics states that the total electric flux through a closed surface enclosing a net charge q is equal to:
(A) q / ε0
(B) ε0 / q
(C) q ε0
(D) 1 / (4πε0) × q
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (A)
According to Gauss's Law, the total electric flux out of any closed surface is equal to the net charge enclosed divided by the permittivity of free space (ε0). Mathematically, ∮ E • dA = qenclosed / ε0.
14.
What is the total electric flux passing through a closed surface if an electric dipole is placed completely inside it?
(A) q / ε0
(B) 2q / ε0
(C) Zero
(D) Infinity
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
An electric dipole consists of a positive charge (+q) and an equal negative charge (-q). The net charge enclosed by the surface is Qnet = +q - q = 0. According to Gauss's law (Φ = Qnet / ε0), the total flux will be zero.
15.
A charge q is enclosed by a spherical Gaussian surface of radius R. If the radius of the sphere is halved, the electric flux passing through the surface will:
(A) Become four times
(B) Become half
(C) Become double
(D) Remain exactly the same
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (D)
Gauss's law (Φ = q / ε0) shows that the total electric flux passing through a closed surface depends only on the charge enclosed by it. It does not depend on the size or shape of the Gaussian surface. Thus, changing the radius has no effect on the flux.
16.
A point charge q is placed precisely at the center of a cube. The electric flux passing through any ONE single face of the cube is:
(A) q / ε0
(B) q / (6ε0)
(C) q / (4ε0)
(D) q / (8ε0)
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
By Gauss's law, the total flux passing through the entire closed cube is q / ε0. Since the charge is placed exactly at the center, the flux is distributed symmetrically and equally among all 6 faces of the cube. Therefore, the flux through one face is (1/6) × (q / ε0).
17.
Which of the following is an acceptable SI unit for Electric Flux?
(A) Newton meter squared per Coulomb (N m2 C-1)
(B) Newton meter per Coulomb (N m C-1)
(C) Volt per meter (V/m)
(D) Joule per Coulomb (J/C)
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (A)
Electric flux Φ = E × A. The unit of Electric field (E) is Newton/Coulomb (N/C), and the unit of Area (A) is square meters (m2). Multiplying these gives the unit of electric flux as N m2 C-1.
18.
A closed Gaussian surface encloses three charges: +3q, -2q, and +q. The net outward electric flux through the surface is:
(A) 6q / ε0
(B) 4q / ε0
(C) 2q / ε0
(D) Zero
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
To find the net flux, we first calculate the net algebraic sum of the charges enclosed.
Qnet = (+3q) + (-2q) + (+q) = +2q.
By Gauss's law, total flux Φ = Qnet / ε0 = 2q / ε0.
19.
Gauss's law is essentially a direct consequence of which fundamental law of physics?
(A) Ampere's Law
(B) Coulomb's Law (Inverse Square Law)
(C) Faraday's Law
(D) Ohm's Law
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
Gauss's law is deeply connected to Coulomb's law. Specifically, it is a consequence of the fact that the electric force (and thus electric field) obeys an inverse square law (E ∝ 1/r2). The r2 in the denominator perfectly cancels the r2 dependency of the surface area (A = 4πr2), making the total flux constant for a given charge.
20.
For maximum electric flux through a given planar area A in a uniform electric field E, the angle between the electric field lines and the AREA VECTOR must be:
(A) 0°
(B) 45°
(C) 90°
(D) 180°
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (A)
Electric flux Φ = EA cosθ, where θ is the angle between the electric field E and the area vector A (which is perpendicular to the plane surface). For flux to be maximum, cosθ must be 1. This happens when θ = 0°. (Note: This means the field lines are perpendicular to the surface itself).
SECTION C: Applications of Gauss's Law
21.
The electric field E at a perpendicular distance r from an infinitely long straight charged wire with linear charge density λ is given by:
(A) E = λ / (πε0r)
(B) E = λ / (2πε0r)
(C) E = λ / (4πε0r2)
(D) E = 2λ / (πε0r2)
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
This is a standard application of Gauss's Law. Drawing a cylindrical Gaussian surface of radius r and length l, the flux is E × 2πrl. The enclosed charge is λl. By Gauss's Law, E(2πrl) = λl / ε0. Solving for E yields E = λ / (2πε0r).
22.
What is the magnitude of the electric field inside a uniformly charged thin spherical conducting shell?
(A) Constant but non-zero
(B) Directly proportional to the radius
(C) Zero everywhere inside
(D) Infinity at the center
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
For any conducting spherical shell, the charge resides strictly on its outer surface. If we draw a spherical Gaussian surface inside the shell (r < R), the charge enclosed (Qenclosed) is zero. Therefore, E(4πr2) = 0, which means the electric field E is strictly zero everywhere inside.
23.
The electric field produced by a uniformly charged infinite plane sheet of surface charge density σ is:
(A) Dependent linearly on the distance from the sheet
(B) Uniform and independent of the distance from the sheet
(C) Inversely proportional to the square of the distance
(D) Zero everywhere outside the sheet
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
Using Gauss's law for a pillbox Gaussian surface, we get the expression E = σ / (2ε0). Notice that the parameter for distance (r or x) does not appear in this formula. This proves that the electric field is perfectly uniform and independent of the distance from the infinite sheet.
24.
Which of the following expressions correctly gives the electric field due to a uniformly charged infinite plane sheet?
(A) E = σ / ε0
(B) E = σ / (2ε0)
(C) E = 2σ / ε0
(D) E = σ / (4πε0)
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (B)
For a thin infinite non-conducting sheet, the flux comes out from both sides of the Gaussian pillbox. Total flux Φ = EA + EA = 2EA. Charge enclosed = σA. By Gauss's Law, 2EA = σA / ε0. Therefore, E = σ / (2ε0).
25.
For a uniformly charged thin spherical shell of radius R and total charge Q, the electric field at a point strictly outside the shell (distance r > R from center) is mathematically equivalent to the field produced by:
(A) An infinite plane sheet of charge
(B) An infinitely long straight charged wire
(C) The entire charge Q concentrated precisely at the center
(D) An electric dipole placed at the center
[4 Marks]
Solution: Correct Option (C)
Using a spherical Gaussian surface of radius r (where r > R), the charge enclosed is the total charge Q of the shell. E(4πr2) = Q / ε0 ⇒ E = Q / (4πε0r2). This is identical to the formula for the electric field produced by a point charge Q located at the origin/center. Thus, for outside points, a spherical shell behaves as if all its charge is concentrated at its geometric center.

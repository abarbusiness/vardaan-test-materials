Class: 7 CBSE |Minor Test 04| Subject: Science (Acids, Bases & Salts) | Max Marks: 50
SECTION A (10 Marks)
Q1. What are organic acids? Give any one example of an organic acid.
[2 Marks]
Answer: Acids which are naturally occurring and are found in plants and animals are called organic acids.
Example: Citric acid (in oranges/lemons), Lactic acid (in sour milk), or Acetic acid (in vinegar). (Any one example)
Q2. Name any two naturally occurring indicators used to test whether a substance is acidic or basic.
[2 Marks]
Answer: Any two of the following:
  • Litmus (obtained from lichens)
  • Turmeric
  • Juice of China rose (Hibiscus) petals
Q3. What happens when an acid reacts with a base? What is this chemical reaction called?
[2 Marks]
Answer: When an acid reacts with a base, they 'cancel' the effect of each other, resulting in the formation of water and salt. This chemical reaction is known as a neutralisation reaction.
Q4. State any two general properties of salts.
[2 Marks]
Answer:
  1. Most of the salts are readily soluble in water.
  2. Salts do not conduct electricity in their solid state, but they conduct electricity in their molten state and in the form of their solution in water.
Q5. Define alkalies. Give one example of an alkali.
[2 Marks]
Answer: Bases which are soluble in water are called alkalies.
Example: Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) or Aluminium hydroxide.
SECTION B (15 Marks)
Q6. Differentiate between mineral acids and organic acids. Give one example of each.
[3 Marks]
Answer:
  • Mineral Acids: Acids which are prepared from the minerals present in the earth are called mineral acids. Example: Hydrochloric acid (HCl) or Sulphuric acid (H2SO4).
  • Organic Acids: Acids which are naturally occurring and found in plants and animals are called organic acids. Example: Citric acid or Acetic acid.
Q7. Classify the following salts as neutral, acidic, or basic salts: (a) Sodium chloride (b) Aluminium chloride (c) Sodium acetate
[3 Marks]
Answer:
  • (a) Sodium chloride (NaCl) - Neutral Salt (formed by strong acid + strong base)
  • (b) Aluminium chloride (AlCl3) - Acidic Salt (formed by strong acid + weak base)
  • (c) Sodium acetate (CH3COONa) - Basic Salt (formed by weak acid + strong base)
Q8. Identify the organic acid present in the following items: (a) Tamarind (b) Vinegar (c) Sour milk
[3 Marks]
Answer:
  • (a) Tamarind contains Tartaric acid.
  • (b) Vinegar contains Acetic acid.
  • (c) Sour milk contains Lactic acid.
Q9. Write the complete chemical equations for the following neutralisation reactions: (a) Sodium hydroxide reacts with hydrochloric acid. (b) Magnesium hydroxide reacts with nitric acid.
[3 Marks]
Answer:
(a) NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H2O
    (Sodium hydroxide) + (Hydrochloric acid) → (Sodium chloride) + (Water)

(b) Mg(OH)2 + 2HNO3 → Mg(NO3)2 + 2H2O
    (Magnesium hydroxide) + (Nitric acid) → (Magnesium nitrate) + (Water)
Q10. What are antacids? How do they help in curing uneasiness and pain in the stomach?
[3 Marks]
Answer: When an excess of hydrochloric acid gets secreted in the stomach, it causes uneasiness, nausea, and pain. To cure this problem, medicines called antacids are taken. Antacids contain a mild base which 'neutralises' the excess acid present in the stomach, providing relief from the pain.
SECTION C (25 Marks)
Q11. (a) Define an indicator. (b) How do Litmus paper and Phenolphthalein change colour in acidic and basic mediums?
[5 Marks]
Answer:
(a) An indicator is a substance which shows different colours in an acidic and a basic medium. They are used to help identify whether a substance is an acid or a base.

(b) Colour changes:
  • Litmus paper: Changes to red in an acidic medium, and changes to blue in a basic medium.
  • Phenolphthalein: Remains colourless in an acidic medium, and turns pink in a basic medium.
Q12. Give scientific reasons: (a) Never taste unknown substances. (b) Turmeric stain turns red with soap. (c) Adding water to concentrated acids.
[5 Marks]
Answer:
  • (a) We must never taste unknown substances because they might contain dangerous chemicals (like strong acids or bases) that are highly corrosive and can cause severe harm or burns to our bodies.
  • (b) Turmeric acts as a natural indicator. It remains yellow in an acidic medium but turns red in a basic medium. Since soap is a base (contains basic chemicals), it turns the yellow turmeric stain into a red colour.
  • (c) Acids manufactured in factories are highly concentrated with very low water content. To decrease their concentration and make them safer to use (dilute acids), water is added to them.
Q13. Write the general names given to the salts formed by the following acids: (a) Sulphuric acid (b) Hydrochloric acid (c) Nitric acid (d) Carbonic acid (e) Acetic acid
[5 Marks]
Answer:
  • (a) Salts of sulphuric acid (H2SO4) are named as sulphates.
  • (b) Salts of hydrochloric acid (HCl) are named as chlorides.
  • (c) Salts of nitric acid (HNO3) are named as nitrates.
  • (d) Salts of carbonic acid (H2CO3) are named as carbonates.
  • (e) Salts of acetic acid (CH3COOH) are named as acetates.
Q14. Describe an activity to show that solutions of salts in water can conduct electricity. Include the materials required and the observation.
[5 Marks]
Answer:
Materials Required: A beaker, water, common salt (sodium chloride), a battery, a zero watt LED bulb, connecting wires, and two graphite rods.
Procedure:
  1. Take a beaker and fill it half with water.
  2. Dissolve some common salt (sodium chloride) in this water to make a salt solution.
  3. Connect the two graphite rods with the two terminals of a battery, placing the zero watt LED bulb in between the circuit.
  4. Dip these graphite rods into the solution of sodium chloride.
Observation & Conclusion: The LED bulb starts glowing immediately. This glowing indicates the flow of electric current through the circuit, proving that a solution of sodium chloride (salt) in water can successfully conduct electricity.
Q15. (a) "All alkalies are bases but all bases are not alkalies." Justify this statement. (b) Give two examples of strong bases and two examples of weak bases.
[5 Marks]
Answer:
(a) Justification: Bases are bitter-tasting substances that react with acids to form water and salt. Alkalies are a specific subset of bases that are soluble in water. Therefore, every alkali is inherently a base (because it is just a water-soluble base). However, not all bases can dissolve in water; the ones that do not dissolve are bases but cannot be called alkalies. Hence, all alkalies are bases, but all bases are not alkalies.

(b) Examples:
  • Strong Bases: Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and Potassium hydroxide (KOH).
  • Weak Bases: Ammonium hydroxide (NH4OH), Aluminium hydroxide [Al(OH)3], or Copper hydroxide [Cu(OH)2]. (Any two)

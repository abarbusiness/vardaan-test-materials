Q3. Define 'Intermolecular force of attraction'. State briefly how it varies among solids, liquids, and gases.
Intermolecular force of attraction: The force of attraction existing between the constituent particles (atoms, molecules, or ions) of matter is called intermolecular force.

Variation: It is strongest (maximum) in solids, intermediate (weaker than solids) in liquids, and weakest (negligible) in gases.

Q8. Explain the process of melting of a solid into a liquid on the basis of the kinetic theory of matter.
When a solid is heated, its particles absorb heat energy, which increases their kinetic energy.
[1.0]
Due to higher kinetic energy, the particles start vibrating more vigorously about their mean positions. At the melting point, this energy becomes sufficient to overcome the strong intermolecular forces of attraction.
[1.0]
As a result, the particles leave their fixed positions, intermolecular spaces increase, and they begin to flow, converting the solid state into a liquid state.
[1.0]
Q9. Give scientific reasons: (a) Solids have a fixed shape and volume. (b) Gases exert pressure in all directions.
(a) In solids, particles are closely packed due to very strong intermolecular forces of attraction. They have negligible intermolecular spaces and can only vibrate in fixed positions. Thus, they cannot flow and maintain a fixed shape and volume.
[1.5]
(b) Gas particles have very weak intermolecular forces and very high kinetic energy. They move continuously and randomly at high speeds in all directions, colliding with each other and with the walls of the container. These continuous collisions exert a force per unit area, resulting in gas pressure.
[1.5]

Q14. State the five main postulates of the kinetic molecular theory of matter.
The main postulates are:
1. Composition: All matter is composed of very small particles called atoms, molecules, or ions.
[1.0]
2. Intermolecular Space: Empty spaces exist between these particles, known as intermolecular spaces.
[1.0]
3. Intermolecular Force: Particles of matter attract each other with a force called intermolecular force of attraction (cohesive or adhesive).
[1.0]
4. Particle Motion (Kinetic Energy): The particles are in a state of continuous random motion and hence possess kinetic energy.
[1.0]
5. Effect of Temperature: The kinetic energy of the particles is directly proportional to the absolute temperature (heating increases particle motion).
[1.0]

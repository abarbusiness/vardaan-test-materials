Q2. Evaluate: \( \log_2 16 + \log_3 27 \)
[2]
Solution:
Expressing arguments as powers of respective bases: \( 16 = 2^4 \) and \( 27 = 3^3 \).
\( = \log_2 (2^4) + \log_3 (3^3) \) [1 Mark]
Using property \( \log_a (a^n) = n \):
\( = 4 \log_2 2 + 3 \log_3 3 \)
\( = 4(1) + 3(1) = 4 + 3 = 7 \) [1 Mark]
Q3. If \( \log_x 64 = \frac{3}{2} \), determine the value of \( x \).
[2]
Solution:
Convert to exponential form:
\( x^{\frac{3}{2}} = 64 \) [1 Mark]
Raise both sides to the power of \( \frac{2}{3} \) to isolate \( x \):
\( (x^{\frac{3}{2}})^{\frac{2}{3}} = (64)^{\frac{2}{3}} \)
\( x = (4^3)^{\frac{2}{3}} = 4^2 \)
\( x = 16 \) [1 Mark]
Q4. Express as a single logarithm: \( 2 \log 3 - \frac{1}{2} \log 16 + \log 12 \)
[2]
Solution:
Use power law \( n \log a = \log (a^n) \):
\( = \log (3^2) - \log (16^{\frac{1}{2}}) + \log 12 \)
\( = \log 9 - \log 4 + \log 12 \) [1 Mark]
Using Product and Quotient Laws:
\( = \log \left( \frac{9 \times 12}{4} \right) \)
\( = \log (9 \times 3) = \log 27 \) [1 Mark]
Q5. Solve for \( x \): \( \log_{10} (x^2 - 21) = 2 \)

\( x = \pm 11 \) [1 Mark]
Q6. Prove: \( \log \left( \frac{81}{8} \right) - 2 \log \left( \frac{3}{2} \right) + 3 \log \left( \frac{2}{3} \right) + \log \left( \frac{3}{4} \right) = 0 \)
[3]
Solution:
LHS: Use power law for the coefficients:
\( = \log \left( \frac{81}{8} \right) - \log \left( \left(\frac{3}{2}\right)^2 \right) + \log \left( \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^3 \right) + \log \left( \frac{3}{4} \right) \) [1 Mark]
\( = \log \left( \frac{81}{8} \right) - \log \left( \frac{9}{4} \right) + \log \left( \frac{8}{27} \right) + \log \left( \frac{3}{4} \right) \)
Combine using Quotient (\(-\)) and Product (\(+\)) laws:
\( = \log \left( \frac{81}{8} \div \frac{9}{4} \times \frac{8}{27} \times \frac{3}{4} \right) \) [1 Mark]
\( = \log \left( \frac{81}{8} \times \frac{4}{9} \times \frac{8}{27} \times \frac{3}{4} \right) \)
Simplifying the fraction:
Numerators: \( 81 \times 4 \times 8 \times 3 = 7776 \)
Denominators: \( 8 \times 9 \times 27 \times 4 = 7776 \)
Ratio = \( 1 \)
\( = \log (1) = 0 = \text{RHS} \). Hence Proved. [1 Mark]
Q7. If \( a^2 + b^2 = 7ab \), prove \( 2 \log (a + b) = 2 \log 3 + \log a + \log b \)
[3]
Solution:
Given: \( a^2 + b^2 = 7ab \)
Add \( 2ab \) to both sides to complete the square:
\( a^2 + b^2 + 2ab = 7ab + 2ab \)
\( (a + b)^2 = 9ab \) [1 Mark]
Taking logarithm on both sides:
\( \log (a + b)^2 = \log (9ab) \) [1 Mark]
Applying power law on LHS, and product law on RHS:
\( 2 \log (a + b) = \log 9 + \log a + \log b \)
\( 2 \log (a + b) = \log (3^2) + \log a + \log b \)
\( 2 \log (a + b) = 2 \log 3 + \log a + \log b \). Hence Proved. [1 Mark]
Q8. Given \( \log_{10} x = a \) and \( \log_{10} y = b \), express \( 10^{2a-b} \) in terms of \( x \) and \( y \).
[3]
Solution:
Convert the given log equations to exponential forms:
\( \log_{10} x = a \implies 10^a = x \) [1 Mark]
\( \log_{10} y = b \implies 10^b = y \) [0.5 Marks]
Now consider the expression: \( 10^{2a-b} \)
Using exponent rules (\( m^{x-y} = \frac{m^x}{m^y} \) and \( m^{xy} = (m^x)^y \)):
\( = \frac{10^{2a}}{10^b} = \frac{(10^a)^2}{10^b} \) [1 Mark]
Substituting \( 10^a = x \) and \( 10^b = y \):
\( = \frac{x^2}{y} \) [0.5 Marks]
Q9. Solve: \( \log x + \log y = 2 \) and \( x - y = 15 \)
[5]
Solution:
From the first equation: \( \log x + \log y = 2 \)
Applying product law (assuming base 10):
\( \log_{10} (xy) = 2 \)
Convert to exponential form:
\( xy = 10^2 \implies xy = 100 \) ---- (Eq. 1) [1.5 Marks]
From the second equation: \( x - y = 15 \implies x = 15 + y \) ---- (Eq. 2)
Substitute (Eq. 2) into (Eq. 1):
\( (15 + y)y = 100 \) [1 Mark]
\( y^2 + 15y - 100 = 0 \)
Factorizing the quadratic equation:
\( y^2 + 20y - 5y - 100 = 0 \)
\( y(y + 20) - 5(y + 20) = 0 \)
\( (y + 20)(y - 5) = 0 \) [1.5 Marks]
Therefore, \( y = -20 \) or \( y = 5 \).
Since logarithms are only defined for positive real numbers, \( x > 0 \) and \( y > 0 \). Thus, reject \( y = -20 \).
So, \( y = 5 \). Substitute \( y = 5 \) in \( x = 15 + y \implies x = 15 + 5 = 20 \).
Final Answer: \( x = 20, y = 5 \) [1 Mark]
SECTION B: SCIENCE (Physics) - 25 Marks
Q10. Body moving with uniform velocity of \( 10 \text{ m/s} \). What is acceleration? Justify.
[2]
Solution:
Acceleration is \( 0 \text{ m/s}^2 \). [1 Mark]
Justification: Acceleration is defined as the rate of change of velocity (\( a = \frac{v-u}{t} \)). Since the velocity is uniform (constant), there is no change in velocity (\( v = u = 10 \text{ m/s} \)). Therefore, the acceleration is zero. [1 Mark]
Q11. Define inertia. On what physical quantity does it depend?
[2]
Solution:
Inertia: It is the inherent property of a body by virtue of which it resists any change in its state of rest or of uniform motion in a straight line. [1 Mark]
Dependency: The inertia of a body depends solely on its Mass. (Greater mass means greater inertia). [1 Mark]
Q12. Stone dropped from a cliff. Velocity after \( 3\text{s} \)? (\( g=10\text{m/s}^2 \))
[2]
Solution:
Initial velocity \( u = 0 \text{ m/s} \) (since it is dropped).
Acceleration \( a = g = 10 \text{ m/s}^2 \), Time \( t = 3 \text{ s} \). [1 Mark]
Using first equation of motion: \( v = u + gt \)
\( v = 0 + (10 \times 3) = 30 \text{ m/s} \). [1 Mark]
Q13. State two differences between distance and displacement.
[2]
Solution:
1. Nature: Distance is a scalar quantity (only magnitude), whereas Displacement is a vector quantity (magnitude and direction). [1 Mark]
2. Values: Distance is always positive for a moving body. Displacement can be positive, negative, or zero. (Alternatively: Distance is the actual path length, displacement is the shortest path). [1 Mark]
Q14. Derive algebraically: \( v^2 = u^2 + 2as \)
[3]
Solution:
Let an object move with initial velocity \( u \), uniform acceleration \( a \), and reach final velocity \( v \), covering distance \( s \) in time \( t \).
From the first equation of motion: \( v = u + at \implies t = \frac{v - u}{a} \) ---- (Eq. 1) [1 Mark]
We know, Distance = Average Velocity \( \times \) Time
\( s = \left( \frac{v + u}{2} \right) \times t \) [1 Mark]
Substitute the value of \( t \) from Eq. 1 into this equation:
\( s = \left( \frac{v + u}{2} \right) \times \left( \frac{v - u}{a} \right) \)
Using identity \( (a+b)(a-b) = a^2 - b^2 \):
\( s = \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2a} \)
\( 2as = v^2 - u^2 \implies v^2 = u^2 + 2as \) (Proved) [1 Mark]
Q15. Bullet \( 20\text{g} \) at \( 150\text{m/s} \) stops in \( 0.03\text{s} \). Calculate force.
[3]
Solution:
Given: Mass \( m = 20 \text{ g} = \frac{20}{1000} \text{ kg} = 0.02 \text{ kg} \). [0.5 Marks]
Initial velocity \( u = 150 \text{ m/s} \), Final velocity \( v = 0 \text{ m/s} \), Time \( t = 0.03 \text{ s} \).
Calculate acceleration \( a = \frac{v - u}{t} \):
\( a = \frac{0 - 150}{0.03} = \frac{-150}{0.03} = -5000 \text{ m/s}^2 \) [1.5 Marks]
Force \( F = ma = 0.02 \text{ kg} \times (-5000 \text{ m/s}^2) \)
\( F = -100 \text{ N} \). The magnitude of the retarding force is 100 N. [1 Mark]
Q16. Car accelerates \( 18 \text{km/h} \) to \( 36 \text{km/h} \) in \( 5\text{s} \). Find (i) \( a \), (ii) distance \( s \).
[3]
Solution:
Initial velocity \( u = 18 \text{ km/h} = 18 \times \frac{5}{18} = 5 \text{ m/s} \).
Final velocity \( v = 36 \text{ km/h} = 36 \times \frac{5}{18} = 10 \text{ m/s} \). Time \( t = 5 \text{ s} \). [1 Mark]
(i) Acceleration (a):
\( a = \frac{v - u}{t} = \frac{10 - 5}{5} = \frac{5}{5} = 1 \text{ m/s}^2 \). [1 Mark]
(ii) Distance (s):
Using \( s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \):
\( s = (5 \times 5) + \frac{1}{2} \times 1 \times (5)^2 \)
\( s = 25 + 12.5 = 37.5 \text{ meters} \). [1 Mark]
Q17. Newton's Third Law & Application to Swimming.
[3]
Solution:
Third Law: To every action, there is always an equal and opposite reaction. (Action and reaction forces act on two different bodies). [1.5 Marks]
Application (Swimming): While swimming, a person pushes the water backward with their hands and feet (Action force). According to Newton's Third Law, the water exerts an equal and opposite forward force on the person (Reaction force), which enables the swimmer to move forward. [1.5 Marks]
Q18. (a) Law of conservation of momentum. (b) Boy \( 40\text{kg} \), \( 5\text{m/s} \) jumps on cart \( 10\text{kg} \). Find \( v \).
[5]
Solution:
(a) Law of Conservation of Momentum: It states that in an isolated system (when no external unbalanced force acts on it), the total momentum of the system remains constant before and after collision. [2 Marks]
(b) Numerical: Let the boy be object 1 and cart be object 2.
Mass of boy \( m_1 = 40 \text{ kg} \), Initial velocity \( u_1 = 5 \text{ m/s} \).
Mass of cart \( m_2 = 10 \text{ kg} \), Initial velocity \( u_2 = 0 \text{ m/s} \) (stationary).
Total initial momentum \( = m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 \)
\( = (40 \times 5) + (10 \times 0) = 200 + 0 = 200 \text{ kg m/s} \) [1 Mark]
After jumping, they move together with a common velocity, let's call it \( v \).
Total final mass \( M = m_1 + m_2 = 40 + 10 = 50 \text{ kg} \).
Total final momentum \( = M \times v = 50v \).
By law of conservation of momentum:
Initial Momentum = Final Momentum
\( 200 = 50v \) [1 Mark]
\( v = \frac{200}{50} = 4 \text{ m/s} \)
The common velocity of the cart and the boy is \( 4 \text{ m/s} \) in the direction the boy was running. [1 Mark]
